## Story Arc

Ethan Evans grew up on a farm in Ohio, became a software engineer, and in his very first job went from fresh-out-of-grad-school to managing a team of 10 in just two years. How? He kept seeing unmet needs and asking his manager how he could fill them. He repeated this pattern at every company — promoted to VP before 30 at his third job. At Amazon, his responsibilities grew from an initial team of 6 to over 800 people across 15 years. He helped create Prime Video, Kindle, Alexa, Twitch, and Prime Gaming. He holds 70+ patents and helped draft Amazon's "Ownership" leadership principle. He retired at 50.

The framework crystallized when Evans started coaching others. He distilled what he'd always done instinctively into five explicit steps — The Magic Loop. He shared it with his mentees, his direct reports, his coaching clients. The results were so consistent and so dramatic that people started writing back asking, "How do I turn this off? I'm in over my head now — my boss keeps giving me cool things, I've already been promoted once, and I need time to digest it." One of his best people joined as an SDE II (level 5) at Amazon, grew through the loop to senior engineer, switched to management, opened a new office, became a director, eventually left to found a startup, sold it, and now works as an EVP at a major online bank. An entry-level person applied it and got a $30,000 raise offer — then turned it down because another company offered even more.

The turning point is the realization that the loop puts YOU in control. You don't need a perfect manager. You don't need a fast-growing company (though it helps). You just need to stop treating your manager like a high school teacher you're trying to dodge homework from, and start treating them like a partner. The magic isn't mysterious — it's basic human reciprocity. Help someone, and they'll help you back.

## Emotional Core

This piece captures the deep tension between feeling stuck in your career and realizing that YOU are the one who can unlock it. It's about the frustration of watching others get promoted while you do "good work" and wait. The emotional breakthrough is that advancement isn't about being noticed — it's about being useful. There's something almost counterintuitive and vulnerable about it: you have to go to your boss and genuinely ask, "How can I help YOU?" — not "How can I get promoted?" The magic is that helping them IS how you get promoted. It taps into the universal feeling of wanting to matter at work, wanting someone to see your potential, and the relief of discovering that the system isn't as rigged as it feels — you just needed the right approach. The "how do I turn this off?" anecdote is pure emotional gold: a framework so powerful its users had to slow it down.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

If you help your boss succeed first, your own career takes off — and the loop of mutual investment compounds faster than you'd believe.
