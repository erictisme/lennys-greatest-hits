## Story Arc

The piece opens with a deceptively simple question: "How do strategy, vision, mission, goals, and roadmap all work together?" Every PM has heard these words thrown around interchangeably in meetings — and felt the confusion. Lenny cuts through it with a clean cascade: Mission → Vision → Strategy → Goals → Roadmap → Task. Six words. One waterfall. Everything connects.

The genius move is the teaching vehicle. After defining each layer with real company examples (Tesla, TED, Stripe, Nike), Lenny doesn't stop at corporate examples. He maps the entire framework onto *Ocean's Eleven* — the heist movie. Danny Ocean's mission: rob three casinos. His vision: great wealth and get Tess back. Strategy: get into the cage, through coded doors, past guards with Uzis, into the vault. Goal: walk out with $150 million. Roadmap: build a team of 11, do recon, hack security. Task: arrive at the casino. It's the same cascade, just with George Clooney.

The turning point is the realization that this isn't just an org chart exercise — it's the PM prayer. Every task you work on should trace back, link by link, all the way up to the mission. If you can't draw that line, you're working on the wrong thing. The framework transforms abstract corporate strategy into something visceral and actionable.

## Emotional Core

This piece taps into the universal feeling of being lost in the weeds — doing tasks without knowing why they matter. Every knowledge worker has felt that disconnect: you're shipping features, hitting sprints, checking boxes, but you can't explain how your work connects to anything bigger. The cascade is the antidote. It gives you permission to zoom out and demand clarity. The Ocean's Eleven analogy makes it fun instead of corporate — it says strategy can be thrilling, like planning a heist. The emotional arc goes from "confused and overwhelmed by buzzwords" to "I see the whole picture now." It's the satisfaction of watching a complex machine click into place.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

Every task you do should trace back to a mission — if you can't draw the line from what you're doing today to why your team exists, you're working on the wrong thing.

## Additional Notes
- **The cascade as a chant/rap structure:** The six words (Mission → Vision → Strategy → Goals → Roadmap → Task) could work as a descending musical motif — each step getting more concrete, like zooming in from space to street level. Think of it as a countdown.
- **The Ocean's Eleven angle is gold for songwriting.** A heist metaphor for product strategy is inherently cinematic and fun. Could structure the whole song as "planning a heist" where the heist is building a product.
- **Connection to other Lenny content:** Links directly to his pieces on product strategy, prioritization, goal-setting, and North Star metrics — this is the umbrella framework that connects many of his most popular posts.
- **"The PM Prayer" angle:** The cascade could be framed as a daily ritual — before you start any work, trace it back. Almost spiritual in its simplicity.
- **Musical direction:** Could work as a hip-hop track (the cascade as a rap verse), an anthemic pop-rock track (building momentum through the six layers), or even a spoken-word intro that drops into a beat.
- **TED's "Spread ideas" as a two-word mission** is a great lyrical contrast moment — sometimes the biggest things are the simplest.
