## Story Arc

Shreyas Doshi joined Google in 2008 as a relatively new PM — and for three straight years, he was overwhelmed and stressed. He was in a high-performance environment, working on important products and launches, with an endless to-do list that never seemed to shrink. He was a perfectionist, pouring the same intensity into every single task. He'd come home and complain to his wife about not making progress. He couldn't sleep. He was producing output, but he felt like he was drowning.

Then everything changed. He found a random blog post (one he can never find again) that planted the seed for what became the LNO Framework. The core insight: not all tasks are created equal. There are three types — **Leverage** tasks (put in 1X effort, get 10X-100X impact), **Neutral** tasks (put in 1X, get ~1.1X back), and **Overhead** tasks (put in effort, get back far less in impact). The epiphany was that ambitious, perfectionist people treat all three types the same — and that's the trap.

The framework freed Shreyas. He started identifying the few L tasks on his list and giving them his best energy at his most productive time of day. For N and O tasks, he learned to do "just enough" — good enough, not perfect. The example he gives is meeting notes: if it's a routine standup, just fire off the action items immediately. But if it's a product review with the CEO on a contentious decision? Spend two hours perfecting those notes — that's an L task. The liberation wasn't working less. It was working *differently* — letting his inner perfectionist shine only where it actually mattered.

## Emotional Core

This is a song about the tyranny of the to-do list — and the liberation of realizing you've been playing the game wrong. Every ambitious person knows the feeling: you work all day, you're exhausted, and yet you feel like you accomplished nothing. You can't sleep because the list is still there, mocking you. You're a perfectionist giving 100% to things that deserve 20%. The emotional arc goes from drowning → epiphany → freedom. The tension is between the seductive lie of "all work matters equally" and the uncomfortable truth that most of what fills your day is noise. The relief isn't working less — it's the permission to be strategically imperfect. To let the meeting notes be "good enough." To save your fire for the moments that actually move the needle 10X or 100X. It's a song about the most freeing word in productivity: *enough*.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

Not every task deserves your best effort — figure out which few tasks give you 10X returns, and let everything else be "good enough."

## Additional Notes
- **Cross-references:** The LNO framework connects strongly to the "Shreyas Doshi Live" episode at the Lenny & Friends Summit, where Shreyas revisits time management for PMs. Also ties to Kevin Yien's episode where Lenny references LNO as his go-to prioritization advice.
- **Cultural moment:** This framework went viral on Twitter/X after the episode aired. Shreyas built a massive following partly on the strength of frameworks like this one.
- **Song production note:** The L-N-O acronym is a gift for songwriting — it's three syllables, rhythmic, and can be chanted. Consider a call-and-response structure: "What kind of task?" / "It's an L!" or a sorting-hat style verse where tasks get categorized.
- **The lost blog post** is a great narrative device — the idea that a life-changing framework came from a random blog post that disappeared from the internet. The best ideas find you when you need them.
- **Universal appeal:** This isn't just for PMs. Anyone drowning in work — founders, managers, creatives — will recognize the feeling. The song should transcend tech jargon.
- **Potential bridge:** The "next month" trap — "Trust me, next month I'll have time for strategy" — is a universal self-deception that could make a powerful bridge section.
