## Story Arc

April Dunford didn't study marketing — she studied engineering. Her first job out of school was product marketing at a startup, back when startups "weren't even cool and we just called them small companies." She was assigned to a product that was "kind of a loser" — a desktop SQL database positioned as a "Microsoft Access killer." After barely selling 200 copies, they decided to kill it. April got the job of calling customers to see how upset they'd be.

The first 20 calls were all the same: "Sorry, lady, we don't have that." Then call 21 changed everything. "Your product made me the hero of the sales team!" the customer shouted. He'd installed it on laptops so salespeople could take orders in the field and sync back to the SQL database at the office. "We're doubling sales! That SQL feature was a game changer!" Out of 100 customers, 94 didn't even know they had the product — but 6 had transformed their business with it.

Instead of killing the product, they repositioned their "Microsoft Access killer" as an "embeddable database for mobile devices." It took off. They were acquired. That "failed" product spawned a product family generating hundreds of millions in revenue — and over 20 years later, it still runs on mobile devices all over the world. This origin story became the foundation for April's life's work: a systematic methodology for positioning that she's now used with over 200 companies, including Epic Games, Google, IBM, and Postman.

## Key Numbers & Facts
- April has positioned over **200 companies** — more B2B tech positioning than anyone on the planet
- She led marketing at **7 startups, 6 of which were acquired**
- The "Microsoft Access killer" sold only **200 copies** before repositioning
- Out of 100 customer calls: **94 didn't know they had it, 6 transformed their business**
- In B2B, **~40% of deals are lost to "no decision"** — meaning lost to spreadsheets, pen and paper, or interns
- Enterprise software typically loses **25% of deals to "no decision"** (newsletter version)
- The Janna Systems CRM grew from **under $2M to close to $80M in 18 months** after repositioning
- Janna was acquired by Siebel for **$1.3 billion**
- Siebel had **8,000 employees and $2B revenue** vs. Janna's **couple dozen employees and <$2M revenue**
- **90% of tech companies** that went public over the past 5 years positioned in existing markets (not new categories)
- The 5-step positioning methodology: Competitive Alternatives → Differentiated Features → Value → Target Customers → Market Category
- Postman's positioning journey: took years to arrive at the "obvious" description — "a platform for building and using APIs"

## Emotional Core

This is a story about identity crisis — not knowing what you are. Every founder, every PM, every builder has felt the gut punch of watching someone look at their product and go "...I don't get it." The emotional core is that terrifying gap between what you THINK you are and what customers ACTUALLY need you to be. April's "Microsoft Access killer" story is heartbreaking and triumphant at the same time — 94 people who didn't even know they owned your product, but 6 who called it a game changer. The tension is between the product you built in your head and the product the market actually wants. The relief comes from surrendering your ego to the data: "Maybe we're not who we thought we were. Maybe we're something better."

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

If customers can't instantly understand what you are and why they need you, the problem isn't your product — it's how you're describing it.
