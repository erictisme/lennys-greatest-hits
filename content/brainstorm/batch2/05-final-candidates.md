## 1. "Wartime" — Eoghan McCabe / Intercom

**Source:** podcasts/eoghan-mccabe.md
**Guest:** Eoghan McCabe (CEO, Intercom)
**Album:** Founders

**The Story:** Intercom was about to hit $0 net new ARR — negative growth territory. Then ChatGPT launched. Six weeks later they had a prototype. Eoghan declared wartime: rewrote company values as "a sharp knife to cut out" underperformers, 40% employee turnover. Their AI agent Fin grew from $1M to $12M ARR in year one, targeting $100M. Survived a "soft coup" along the way.

**Chorus Hook Idea:** "We were about to hit zero / Six weeks to build a hero / Wartime, wartime — cut the rope or die"

**Key Numbers:** $0 net new ARR (crisis), 40% turnover, Fin: $1M → $12M → $100M ARR, 6-week prototype

**Best Verbatim Quote:** "We were about to hit $0 net new ARR, which means we would've been in negative growth territory."

**Why It Works as Music:** The wartime/peacetime CEO dichotomy is visceral and dramatic. The $0-to-$100M arc is a resurrection story. The "soft coup" subplot adds intrigue. Every founder knows the moment when survival mode kicks in.

**Vocal Style:** Male vocal, indie rock with urgency (think The National meets Arcade Fire). Tense verses building to explosive chorus. Driving drums, distorted bass.

## 2. "Streak" — Duolingo's Gamification Growth Story

**Source:** newsletters/how-duolingo-reignited-user-growth.md
**Guest:** Jorge Mazal / Sean Colombo (Duolingo growth team)
**Album:** Product

**The Story:** Duolingo's DAU was growing at single digits. Their first gamification experiment (moves counter from Gardenscapes) was a total failure — "completely neutral, the team almost immediately and dramatically disbanded." Then leaderboards, streaks, and notifications drove 4.5x DAU growth over 4 years, supercharging the IPO. They studied a match-3 mobile game for retention inspiration.

**Chorus Hook Idea:** "Don't break the streak / Day one-hundred-forty-three / The owl won't let me sleep / But I can't let it go free"

**Key Numbers:** 4.5x DAU growth in 4 years, single-digit growth → IPO, first experiment = total failure, #1 Lenny post ever

**Best Verbatim Quote:** "Depressingly, the result of all that effort was completely neutral. I was deflated."

**Why It Works as Music:** Everyone knows the Duolingo owl. Streaks are universally understood — the compulsion, the guilt, the green bird haunting your notifications. The failure-then-triumph arc is classic songwriting structure. Light and fun with dark undertones of addiction.

**Vocal Style:** Female vocal, synth-pop / electro-pop (think CHVRCHES). Playful melody, addictive hook that mirrors the streak mechanic. Bright production with anxious undertone.

## 3. "Near Death" — Eric Simons / Bolt

**Source:** podcasts/eric-simons.md
**Guest:** Eric Simons (CEO, Bolt/StackBlitz)
**Album:** Founders

**The Story:** StackBlitz was on the verge of going under when they launched Bolt. In the first 2 months: $0 to $20M ARR. Then $30M+ ARR. A core team of 5-6 people who'd been together 5+ years. Lenny called it "non-stop wild shit." The company was dying, then it was the fastest-growing dev tool ever.

**Chorus Hook Idea:** "We were dying on a Tuesday / Twenty million by the end of May / Five of us against the world / Near death, near death — we wouldn't fold"

**Key Numbers:** $0 to $20M ARR in 2 months, $30M+ ARR, 5-6 core team members, 5+ years together

**Best Verbatim Quote:** "The company was on the verge of going under when we launched Bolt."

**Why It Works as Music:** The near-death-to-rocket-ship arc is the ultimate startup anthem. The tiny team (5-6 people) makes it intimate and relatable. The speed of the turnaround ($0 to $20M in 2 months) creates urgency. This is a song about loyalty and the payoff of sticking together.

**Vocal Style:** Male vocal, alt-rock anthem (think Foo Fighters). Building from quiet desperation to triumphant chorus. Raw energy, big guitars on the hook.

## 4. "Complicit" — Jerry Colonna

**Source:** podcasts/jerry-colonna.md
**Guest:** Jerry Colonna (CEO of Reboot, exec coach, former VC)
**Album:** Human Side

**The Story:** Former VC who co-founded one of the most successful early-stage funds ever, turned executive coach. His signature question: "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?" The word "complicit" is deliberate — think accomplice, not responsible. "You are driving the getaway car, you're not sticking up the bank teller." He calls out how founders are "socialized to bullshit not only ourselves, but everybody else."

**Chorus Hook Idea:** "How have I been complicit / In everything I say I hate? / Driving the getaway car / While I blame it all on fate"

**Key Numbers:** Co-founded a top early-stage VC fund, now CEO of Reboot, coached hundreds of founders

**Best Verbatim Quote:** "We're socialized to bullshit not only ourselves, but everybody else, especially in the entrepreneurial community."

**Why It Works as Music:** This is THE coaching question. One line that breaks people open. The "getaway car" metaphor is inherently cinematic. The song structure writes itself: verse = the conditions you hate, chorus = the realization you helped create them. Deeply personal, universally applicable.

**Vocal Style:** Male vocal, acoustic folk-rock (think Iron & Wine or Bon Iver). Sparse, intimate verses. Chorus opens up with harmonies. Confessional and raw.

## 5. "Freemium Killed Us" — Bobby Pinero / Equals

**Source:** newsletters/lessons-from-going-freemium-a-decision-that-broke-our-business.md
**Guest:** Bobby Pinero (CEO, Equals)
**Album:** Product

**The Story:** Equals launched with extreme friction: onboarding call required, pay upfront, no trial. Revenue took off. Raised $16M Series A from a16z. Then they listened to customers screaming for self-serve and freemium. 4x'd active companies initially... then stalled. Each attempt to reduce friction made things worse. "Just kidding. It was awful." Eventually reversed everything — went back to high friction — and recovered.

**Chorus Hook Idea:** "They said make it easy / So we gave it all for free / Four thousand companies and falling / Freemium killed us — can't you see?"

**Key Numbers:** $16M Series A (a16z), 4x users initially, then stalled, reversed course back to high friction

**Best Verbatim Quote:** "We blew up. Thousands of active companies. Just kidding. It was awful."

**Why It Works as Music:** The "just kidding, it was awful" twist is perfect for a song. The cautionary tale of giving customers what they ask for and it nearly killing you is counterintuitive and memorable. The reversal — going BACK to friction — is the satisfying resolution. Every builder has felt the pressure to make things "easier."

**Vocal Style:** Male vocal, indie pop with a wry edge (think Vampire Weekend or The Strokes). Conversational verses, punchy chorus. The "just kidding" moment should land as a musical surprise.

## 7. "Give Away Your Legos" — Molly Graham

**Source:** podcasts/molly-graham.md
**Guest:** Molly Graham (Google, Facebook, CZI, Quip/Salesforce)
**Album:** Human Side

**The Story:** Worked with Zuckerberg, Sheryl Sandberg, Chamath, Bret Taylor. Her framework: as a company grows, you have to give away the things you've gotten good at and move to the next pile. Chamath drew her a picture: "Most people do careers like stairs. The much more fun careers are like jumping off cliffs — you do fall, but then you climb out way beyond where the stairs could ever get you." Google "felt like two PhD students' paradise." Facebook "felt like a 19-year-old hacker's dorm room."

**Chorus Hook Idea:** "Give away your Legos / Let somebody else build what you made / Jump off the cliff, don't take the stairs / The fall is where the growth gets paid"

**Key Numbers:** Google → Facebook → CZI → Salesforce, hundreds of coached companies

**Best Verbatim Quote:** "You have to grow as fast as your company is growing — learning to give away what you've gotten good at and move on to the next shiny pile of Legos."

**Why It Works as Music:** "Give away your Legos" is already a metaphor that sticks in your brain. Every professional has felt the pain of handing off something they built. Chamath's cliff-vs-stairs visual is inherently musical (falling, climbing). The Lego metaphor makes an abstract career concept concrete and childlike.

**Vocal Style:** Female vocal, folk-pop (think Brandi Carlile or Phoebe Bridgers). Warm, reflective verses about building something. Chorus lifts with the "jump off the cliff" moment. Bittersweet but ultimately empowering.

## 8. "When Enough Is Enough" — Andy Johns

**Source:** podcasts/andy-johns.md (Hidden Gem #1)
**Guest:** Andy Johns (ex-Facebook, Twitter, Quora growth leader)
**Album:** Human Side

**The Story:** Andy spent 17 years stringing together Silicon Valley success — Facebook growth team, Twitter, Quora. At Twitter in 2010, at the peak of his career, he was suddenly hit with near-constant panic attacks and depression. He'd grab his laptop and quietly walk out of the office at 10am. Great poker face — nobody could see it. An "addiction to achievement" that became his identity. He hit rock bottom and had to let the old Andy die. Lenny called it "maybe the most meaningful and important episode of the podcast."

**Chorus Hook Idea:** "My career was at its highest / I was at my lowest / Walked out at ten AM / And nobody noticed"

**Key Numbers:** 17 years in startups, panic attacks at ~age 28, Facebook → Twitter → Quora

**Best Verbatim Quote:** "As my career reached its pinnacle... from a professional, from a financial perspective, I was at my highest, but when it came to other aspects of my life, I was arguably at my lowest."

**Why It Works as Music:** The highest/lowest contrast is a ready-made lyrical device. The image of walking out of the office at 10am while everyone keeps working is cinematic. This is the song for everyone in tech who's ever wondered if the achievement treadmill is worth it. Lenny's own endorsement ("most meaningful episode") gives it weight.

**Vocal Style:** Male vocal, slow-build indie (think Sufjan Stevens or The War on Drugs). Quiet, almost spoken-word verses. The chorus should hit like a wall of sound — the contrast between the quiet exit and the internal screaming.

## 9. "Machetes and Motorcycles" — Kevin Aluwi / Gojek

**Source:** podcasts/kevin-aluwi.md
**Guest:** Kevin Aluwi (co-founder, Gojek)
**Album:** Builders

**The Story:** In Gojek's early days, motorcycle taxi mafias physically assaulted drivers — bricks thrown, machetes brandished. Rather than treating drivers as expendable contractors, Gojek hired private security companies to protect them. They ran "a fairly big private security operation for a fairly long time." This is startup life in Southeast Asia — physical danger, not just market risk. 100% month-over-month growth while dodging actual violence.

**Chorus Hook Idea:** "Bricks and machetes in Jakarta rain / A hundred percent growth month over month / We could've let them fight it on their own / But we chose to bring our people home"

**Key Numbers:** 100% MoM growth, largest startup in Southeast Asia, private security operation for drivers

**Best Verbatim Quote:** "We've had everything from bricks thrown at our drivers to knives and machetes being brandished at them."

**Why It Works as Music:** This is the most visceral, physical startup story in the entire archive. No metaphorical "battles" — actual machetes. The moral choice (protect drivers vs. treat them as expendable) gives the song ethical weight. The contrast with Silicon Valley startup stories (foosball tables vs. bricks) is striking. A completely different kind of builder anthem.

**Vocal Style:** Male vocal, world-influenced rock (think Peter Gabriel or Sting-era Police). Percussive, urgent rhythm. Southeast Asian musical elements. Raw and intense.

## 10. "No KPIs" — Archie Abrams / Shopify

**Source:** podcasts/archie-abrams.md
**Guest:** Archie Abrams (VP Product, Shopify)
**Album:** Builders

**The Story:** Shopify is 10% of all US e-commerce (excluding Amazon/Walmart). $235B GMV in 2023 — "roughly the size of the economy of Finland." They banned KPIs for core product teams, make decisions on taste and intuition, and OPTIMIZE for churn. The counterintuitive logic: lower barriers to entrepreneurship, most businesses fail, but the winners make the whole thing work because Shopify earns from payments tied to merchant success. 100-year company vision.

**Chorus Hook Idea:** "No KPIs, no dashboards on the wall / Just taste and intuition — that's the call / We optimize for churn, let businesses fail / The ones who make it write the fairy tale"

**Key Numbers:** 10% of US e-commerce, $235B GMV (economy of Finland), banned KPIs, 100-year vision

**Best Verbatim Quote:** "Most businesses do ultimately fail... the folks who do go on to be successful will make that entire cohort extremely successful for Shopify."

**Why It Works as Music:** "No KPIs" is the most contrarian statement in the entire Lenny archive. In a world obsessed with measurement, Shopify bets on taste. "Optimize for churn" sounds insane until you understand the logic. The "economy of Finland" comparison makes the scale tangible. This is a builder anthem for everyone sick of dashboard culture.

**Vocal Style:** Male vocal, electronic/alternative (think LCD Soundsystem or Hot Chip). Confident, slightly rebellious energy. Repetitive hook that mirrors the simplicity of the philosophy. Minimalist production that practices what it preaches.
