## Story Arc

The piece opens with Lenny's raw, relatable confession: "If you think you're bad at negotiating, I'm even worse, I promise you. Why can't you just *tell* me how much it'll be, and then we can move on?" This sets up the universal tension — everyone hates negotiating, but everyone else is playing the game, and you're losing if you don't play.

Enter Niya Dragova, who drops a jaw-dropping opener: she helped a female FAANG executive get $5.4 million more on her offer. Even this powerful, admired senior leader "had very little knowledge of how to negotiate." The turning point is the realization that negotiation isn't about being aggressive or greedy — it's a structured game with specific rules. The "aha" is that compensation is "set up to be intentionally misleading" and that "sticking up for yourself is nerve-racking AF" — but there are 10 clear commandments that demystify the whole process.

The arc resolves with empowerment: once you know the rules, you can play. The final commandment debunks six common myths (you need competing offers, you should email your ask, you should be aggressive) and replaces them with a collaborative, strategic approach. The message: don't ask for money — make them give you more by creating obstacles they need to overcome to close you.

## Key Numbers & Facts
- $5.4 million — largest salary increase Niya helped negotiate (for a female FAANG executive)
- Over 80% of people get the "middle of the road" template opening offer
- Women make only 47 cents in equity for every dollar a man makes
- 5.3M offers browsable on Candor's platform for benchmarking
- FAANG companies (5K+ employees) use a separate "compensation committee" to set salary
- Companies use formulaic comp based on background, interview performance, and level
- $100K+ increases are possible when you collaborate with (not threaten) recruiters
- 10 commandments structure — a biblical/authoritative framing

## Emotional Core

This piece taps into a deeply universal anxiety: the stomach-churning moment when someone asks "What are your salary expectations?" and you freeze. It's about the tension between wanting to be liked and wanting to be paid what you're worth. There's shame (am I being greedy?), fear (will they rescind the offer?), and rage (why is this system designed to confuse me?). The emotional release comes from realizing the game has rules, and once you know them, the power shifts. It transforms negotiation from a confrontation into a strategy game — and that reframe is genuinely liberating. The $5.4M story for a woman who "everyone admired" but still didn't know how to play the game hits especially hard — if she needed help, we all do.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

Never give them your number first — the game has rules, and once you learn them, you stop leaving money on the table.
