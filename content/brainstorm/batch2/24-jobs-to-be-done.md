## Story Arc

Bob Moesta is an engineer who was told growing up to "build it and they will come" — the foundational lie of product development. He couldn't read or write due to three head injuries before age seven, was told he'd be a baggage handler or construction worker. But that disability became his superpower: instead of reading market research reports full of vague words like "easy, fast, fun, cheap," he went and talked to people. He discovered that people don't buy products — they *hire* them to make progress in their lives. This became Jobs to Be Done.

The turning point is the Snickers vs. Milky Way story. Two candy bars, nearly identical ingredients, sold in the same aisle. But they're hired for completely different jobs. Snickers is hired when you missed lunch, you're running out of energy, you need to get back to work — it competes with protein shakes and Red Bull. Milky Way is hired after an emotional experience, eaten alone, to regroup — it competes with a glass of wine, a brownie, or a run. If you look at the products, they're competitors. If you look at the *people*, they're in completely different universes. That's the whole framework in one example.

The deepest story is the dining room table. Bob built condos for downsizers — empty nesters leaving 3,000 sq ft homes for 1,500 sq ft condos. They all said "I don't want a dining room table, I'm done hosting holidays." So Bob didn't build space for one. Sales stalled. People would buy, then cancel six weeks later. When he investigated, he discovered the dining room table was the emotional bank account of their entire life — every holiday, every birthday, every family moment lived at that table. They couldn't move until they knew where it would go. So Bob did the opposite of what they told him: he sacrificed the second bedroom to build a space for the table (you couldn't even pull the chairs out). Sales jumped 22%. "They said this, but you did that" — that's Jobs to Be Done.

## Emotional Core

This is a story about the gap between what people say and what people do — and the life-changing power of closing that gap. There's deep human tension in the idea that we don't even understand our own buying decisions. We say we want Energy Star homes but buy finished basements. We say we don't need the dining room table but can't move without it. We say we left our job because we were "lucky" but when you unpack the story, luck had nothing to do with it.

The emotional hook is empathy as a superpower. Bob — a man who literally cannot read — became one of the greatest product thinkers alive by *listening*. Not to what people say, but to the story behind what they did. The feeling is one of revelation: "Oh, THAT'S why people really do what they do." It's the feeling of finally understanding a mystery you didn't even know you were living inside.

There's also something deeply moving about Bob's origin: told he'd be a baggage handler, mentored by giants (Deming, Christensen, Taguchi), painting portraits of his mentors every night because "you wake up the next day with a full bank account of energy and you can't save it."

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

People don't buy your product because of what it does — they hire it because of a struggling moment in their life, and if you don't understand that moment, you'll build the wrong thing every time.
