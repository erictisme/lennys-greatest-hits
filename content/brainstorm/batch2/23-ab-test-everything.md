## Story Arc

Ronny Kohavi is widely regarded as the world's leading expert on A/B testing. He spent his career at the holy trinity of experimentation — Amazon, Microsoft, and Airbnb — running tens of thousands of experiments. His core revelation is deeply humbling: we are terrible at predicting what will work. At Microsoft alone, about two-thirds of ideas failed. At Bing (after years of optimization), the failure rate climbed to 85%. At Airbnb, a staggering 92% of experiments failed. And yet every team that starts experimenting thinks they'll be different. They're all humbled.

The centerpiece story is a legendary Bing experiment. Someone proposed a tiny change to how ads were displayed — move the second line of an ad up to the first line, making the title larger. It was a "meh" idea that languished on the backlog for months. Nobody prioritized it. Finally, one frustrated engineer said "My God, we're spending too much time discussing it. I could just implement it." He built it in a couple of days and launched the experiment. Then an alarm went off — a big escalation that something was wrong with the revenue metric. The team scrambled to find a bug. But there was no bug. That tiny, ignored change had increased Bing's revenue by 12% — worth $100 million. It was the single biggest revenue impact in Bing's entire history. And nobody had predicted it.

The deeper lesson isn't just "test things." It's about intellectual humility — accepting that our intuitions are unreliable, that the smallest changes can have the biggest impact, and that the only way to know is to run the experiment. Kohavi champions "test everything" — every code change, every feature, every bug fix should be in an experiment. He also introduces the concept of the OEC (Overall Evaluation Criterion) — you can't just optimize for revenue, because you'll destroy user experience. You need guardrail metrics that protect what matters while you chase growth. The framework is: be bold in what you try, humble about what you expect, and rigorous about what you measure.

## Key Numbers & Facts
- 66% of ideas fail at Microsoft overall
- 85% failure rate at Bing (a highly optimized product)
- 92% of experiments failed at Airbnb — highest Kohavi ever observed
- 80-90% failure rate reported across Booking, Google Ads, and other major companies
- 10% of experiments are aborted on day one due to bugs
- The Bing ad title change: 12% revenue increase, worth $100 million
- That change was the biggest revenue impact in Bing's history
- Microsoft was running 20,000-25,000 experiments per year (~100 new treatments every working day) when Kohavi left in 2019
- 200,000 users is the threshold where A/B testing becomes "magical" for detecting meaningful effects
- Opening links in a new tab was a massive win at Microsoft (2008), then independently at Airbnb years later

## Emotional Core

This is a song about the gap between confidence and reality — about how our egos convince us we know what will work, and how the data proves us wrong again and again. There's a beautiful tension between the bravado of "I've got a great idea" and the humility of discovering that 80-92% of ideas fail. The Bing story is the emotional climax: a neglected, "meh" idea that an impatient engineer built in two days turns out to be worth $100 million. It's an underdog story about a line of code, not a person. The feeling is one of awe mixed with humility — the universe doesn't care about your roadmap priorities, your executive opinions, or your design instincts. Only the experiment knows. And there's something liberating about that — if most ideas fail, you're free to try anything.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

Don't trust your gut — test it. The ideas you dismiss might be worth millions, and the ones you love might be worth nothing.
