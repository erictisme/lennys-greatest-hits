## Story Arc

**Story 1 — Cascade (the one that didn't make it):** Jake Fuentes and his co-founder started Cascade in 2019 — repeat entrepreneurs, backed by First Round Capital and Redpoint, $5.3M seed round, spectacular team. They built a no-code data tool for nontechnical business analysts stuck in Excel. For four years they pushed — marquee customers, a team of 10 — but growth never broke out. Their ICP was too broad ("people who use Excel" isn't a segment). They targeted Alteryx, a billion-dollar clunky incumbent, thinking modern software would win. But Alteryx's users actually liked it, power users had built careers on it, and switching costs were far deeper than install time — they included politics, emotions, career ambitions, and sheer laziness. Early deals came through founder relationships, not real market pull. "The founding team could push the car forward, but we mistook that movement for a machine that could run on its own. Once we stopped pushing, the car stopped." In 2023, they pursued a soft landing.

**Story 2 — Equals (the one that almost broke):** Bobby Pinero's Equals launched in April 2022 as a next-gen spreadsheet. They started high-friction: mandatory sales call + payment upfront, no trial. Revenue took off. They raised a $16M Series A from a16z in five months. Then everyone screamed "just let us in!" — users, investors, advisors. They went freemium, removed friction, 4x'd users overnight. Then engagement, retention, and revenue tanked. They tried removing more friction — CSV uploads, skipping data source setup — and it got worse each time. The initial surge was pent-up demand; once exhausted, for every new company they gained, one dropped off. The turning point: they killed free, required credit cards upfront, brought back the data source requirement. ARR bounced back *immediately*. Deeply engaged users went UP. Less friction had actually cost them engaged users.

**The turning point across both stories:** The moment founders stopped listening to what people *said* they wanted and started reading what the *data* actually showed. For Cascade, it was realizing that cheering supporters aren't market signal. For Equals, it was seeing that users who skip the hard stuff never come back.

## Key Numbers & Facts

## Emotional Core

This is about the loneliest feeling in startups: doing everything "right" and still losing. Both stories capture founders who had the pedigree, the funding, the team, the thesis — and still watched it slip. Cascade's story is the slow fade, the dawning realization over years that the car only moves when you push it. Equals' story is the self-inflicted wound — listening to the crowd, opening the gates, and watching your best thing break. Together they capture the tension between what people say they want and what actually works, between optimism and data, between pushing forward and knowing when to turn back. The emotional hook is recognition — every builder has felt the moment when the numbers stop lying to you.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

The people cheering for you aren't proof you're winning — only real traction that moves without you pushing is.
