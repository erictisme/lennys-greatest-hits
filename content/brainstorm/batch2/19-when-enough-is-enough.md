## Story Arc

Andy Johns spent 17 years climbing the tech ladder — Facebook, Twitter, Quora, Wealthfront — becoming a legendary growth leader. On the outside, his career was at its pinnacle: VP of Growth, VP of Product, President of Wealthfront, next in line for CEO, then founding partner of a VC firm earning high six to early seven figures. But underneath, he was falling apart. Panic attacks at Twitter at age 28 forced him to quietly slip out of the office at 10 AM. He'd bail on all-hands presentations. His teeth were ground down from stress (rebuilt twice). His heart nearly gave out at 35 — a Stanford cardiologist told him, "You're just going to be another 40-something-year-old CEO with a broken heart."

The turning point: even after the heart attack scare, Andy swept it under the carpet and joined ANOTHER startup — his sixth in a row. Three years later, the walls finally closed in. He spent 45 days in a mental health institute. That's when he traced everything back to its root: his mother was severely mentally ill (bipolar, psychotic episodes, in and out of psychiatric hospitals) and died when he was 10. As a child, he learned the only way to feel lovable was to achieve — score goals, get straight A's, be the captain. That adaptation saved his life as a kid, but by 35 it was destroying him.

Andy walked away from everything — the career, the salary, the identity. He now does mental health advocacy, sits on the board of Heroic Hearts Project (psychedelic therapy for veterans with PTSD), and coaches burned-out high achievers. He describes a four-step transformation: suffer, seek truth, find self-compassion, then extend compassion to others. His new metaphor for life: stop climbing mountains. Float down the river instead. Surrender. Go mummy mode in the rapids. At the time of recording, he was calling from Vietnam, following wherever the current took him.

## Emotional Core

This is the story of a man who achieved everything society told him to want — and discovered it was all built on a 10-year-old's grief. The emotional core is the collision between external success and internal devastation. Andy's body literally broke (heart, teeth, sleep, panic) while his resume gleamed. The deepest tension: the thing that saved him as a child (achievement as love-substitute) became the thing that was killing him as an adult. Walking away from a seven-figure career wasn't giving up — it was the bravest thing he ever did. This is about learning that you are lovable even when you stop performing.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

The alarm your body is screaming — broken sleep, broken heart, broken teeth — is telling you something your ambition won't let you hear: it's time to stop climbing and start living.

## Additional Notes
- The "body keeps the score" reference (Bessel van der Kolk's book) is widely known and could be a powerful lyrical anchor.
- Strong connection to other Lenny burnout content — this could be the emotional centerpiece of the Career Moves album.
- The mountain-to-river metaphor shift is incredibly singable: verse 1 = climbing, verse 2 = the fall, bridge = the river, final chorus = surrender.
- Andy's four-step framework (suffer → seek truth → self-compassion → compassion for others) maps perfectly to a song structure (verse 1 → verse 2 → bridge → outro).
- The Stanford cardiologist line — "another 40-something CEO with a broken heart" — is an absolute killer hook. "Broken heart" works on two levels: cardiac and emotional.
- Consider genre: piano ballad building to anthemic. Think Coldplay "Fix You" energy. Or stripped acoustic with building strings.
- Andy's story about running ultra marathons and crying at the end of every run is incredibly cinematic for a music video or visual element.
- The Thai farmer's "everyone's trying to make it to Bangkok by following somebody else's road" could be a great bridge lyric.
