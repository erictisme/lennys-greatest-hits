# Batch 2 Hidden Gems — Underrated Episodes with Song Potential

Episodes most subscribers haven't listened to but contain emotionally powerful, story-rich material for songs. Selected for: surprising/counterintuitive content, less-known guests with breakthrough stories, Lenny's personal/vulnerable moments, and expert disagreements.

**Criteria:** NOT already in batch 2 deep-reads (02-deep-reads.md). NOT batch 1 topics (PMF, tarpit, taste, 11-star, talk to users, shipping, vibe coding, imposter, burnout, doing things that don't scale).

## 1. Andy Johns — "When Enough Is Enough"

**Source:** podcasts/andy-johns.md (2023-09-10)
**Guest:** Andy Johns (ex-Facebook, Twitter, Quora growth leader)

**The Story:** Andy spent 17 years stringing together Silicon Valley success — Facebook growth team, Twitter, Quora — building an "impressive resume" while his emotional and spiritual health collapsed underneath. At Twitter in 2010, in his late 20s, at the peak of his career, he was suddenly hit with near-constant panic attacks and depression. He'd grab his laptop and quietly walk out of the office at 10am. He bowed out of all-hands presentations. He had a "great poker face" — nobody could see it. He describes an "addiction to achievement" that became his identity. He hit rock bottom and had to let the old Andy — the one whose identity was "entirely attached to succeeding" — die. Lenny calls it "maybe the most meaningful and important episode of the podcast."

**Why It's a Hidden Gem:** This is a human interest episode, not a growth hack. Most Lenny listeners skip it. But the story of Silicon Valley's golden boy secretly falling apart is universally resonant — and the "death of the old me" transformation arc is pure songwriting gold.

**Key Numbers:** 17 years in startups, panic attacks starting at age ~28, walked out of office at 10am multiple times
**Best Quote:** "As my career reached its pinnacle... from a professional, from a financial perspective, I was at my highest, but when it came to other aspects of my life, I was arguably at my lowest."
**Emotional Core:** The gap between external success and internal suffering. The death of an identity.

## 2. Kenneth Berger — "Fired from Slack Three Times"

**Source:** podcasts/kenneth-berger.md (2024-05-19)
**Guest:** Kenneth Berger (exec coach, first PM at Slack)

**The Story:** Kenneth was the first PM at Slack — he joined the tiny team building what would become one of the biggest enterprise products ever. But he was so attached to being right, so overconfident, that he was fired from Slack three times ("which I feel like has to be some kind of startup record"). The first firing came six weeks in — he came back from a backpacking trip to an email: "Hey, you're fired. It seems like it's not working out." He profusely apologized, got his job back on Monday ("early stage startup, turns out firing doesn't always stick"), but things were never the same. The core team had been through layoffs and hard times together reinventing from a game to Slack — they wanted humility, not "supreme overconfidence."

**Why It's a Hidden Gem:** Kenneth isn't a famous CEO. But "fired three times from the same startup" is an unforgettable hook. The lesson — being right matters less than how you show up — is deeply relatable for anyone who's ever been too sure of themselves.

**Key Numbers:** First PM at Slack, fired 3 times, came back from a backpacking trip to a firing email at week 6
**Best Quote:** "I was fired from Slack three different times, which I feel like has to be some kind of startup record."
**Emotional Core:** Humility learned the hardest way. The pain of being right but wrong about everything else.

## 3. Paul Millerd — "The Pathless Path"

**Source:** podcasts/paul-millerd.md (2023-11-19)
**Guest:** Paul Millerd (author of The Pathless Path)

**The Story:** Paul quit the "default path" — the preset life script of school, prestigious job, promotions, retirement. He self-published a book about it that sold 40,000+ copies (top 0.1% of books sold). The episode is deeply personal for Lenny, who says: "What Paul describes in his book is almost exactly the path I took to figure out this very weird and wonderful work that I do now with the newsletter and podcast." Paul's framework: people don't leap into new lives overnight — they "quit jobs after years of awakening and safely testing changes, eliminating risks along the way." The episode asks the fundamental question most people never ask: "Why do you work?"

**Why It's a Hidden Gem:** This is a life philosophy episode, not a tactics episode. Listeners looking for "how to grow 10x" skip it. But it's arguably the most Lenny-specific episode — it's literally Lenny's own origin story reflected through someone else. And "the pathless path" is a beautiful song concept.

**Key Numbers:** 40,000+ copies sold (self-published), top 0.1% of books
**Best Quote:** "A lot of people have never really thought about, why do you work? People say money. Okay, that's fine. But what else? Why?"
**Emotional Core:** The terror and freedom of stepping off the default path. Lenny's own journey.

## 4. Lulu Cheng Meservey — "The Underdog Playbook"

**Source:** podcasts/lulu-cheng-meservey.md (2023-03-23)
**Guest:** Lulu Cheng Meservey (Substack, Activision Blizzard)

**The Story:** Lulu ran comms at Substack during its most controversial period and at Activision Blizzard during its $69B Microsoft acquisition. Her framework for underdogs: "First, you have to acknowledge that you're the underdog." You can't use the GE playbook from the 1980s. You can't rely on mainstream media, government, or institutional backing. Instead: build your own distribution from day one, decrease surface area to increase pressure ("with the same amount of force you can apply more pressure"), and embrace the insurgency mentality. She explicitly says: "If you're a startup, by definition you're trying to disrupt something — you're fighting the status quo."

**Why It's a Hidden Gem:** Comms/PR is an unusual Lenny topic — most subscribers skip it. But the "underdog insurgency" framework is deeply singable and applies to every founder and builder. The David vs. Goliath energy is universal.

**Key Numbers:** Managed comms for $69B acquisition, Substack's rise
**Best Quote:** "If you're a startup, by definition you're trying to disrupt something, you're trying to do something differently, you're fighting the status quo."
**Emotional Core:** The scrappy underdog fighting giants with nothing but conviction and clever strategy.

## 5. Ronny Kohavi — "80% of Your Features Will Fail"

**Source:** podcasts/ronny-kohavi.md (2023-07-27)
**Guest:** Ronny Kohavi (Airbnb, Microsoft, Amazon — world's leading A/B testing expert)

**The Story:** Ronny ran experimentation at Microsoft, Amazon, and Airbnb and has the data to prove something devastating: most features that teams pour their hearts into have zero or negative impact. "Be ready to fail 80% of the time." Even small bug fixes can have "surprising, unexpected impact." At Microsoft, he built the experimentation platform that tested everything — and the results consistently showed that the majority of carefully designed, passionately argued features made no difference. The counterintuitive truth: you can't predict what works. Only the experiment knows.

**Why It's a Hidden Gem:** "A/B testing" sounds boring — most listeners skip this one. But the emotional truth is devastating: 80% of everything you build doesn't matter. It's the perfect counterpoint to the "ship fast" ethos. The song potential is in the existential dread of building something nobody needs.

**Key Numbers:** 80% failure rate for features, tested at Microsoft/Amazon/Airbnb scale
**Best Quote:** "If you go for something big, try it out, but be ready to fail 80% of the time."
**Emotional Core:** The humbling truth that most of what we build doesn't work. The courage to keep building anyway.

## 6. Jessica Livingston — "YC's Secret Weapon: The Social Radar"

**Source:** podcasts/jessica-livingston.md (2024-06-27)
**Guest:** Jessica Livingston (co-founder of Y Combinator)

**The Story:** Paul Graham's own words: "Much of what's novel about YC is due to Jessica Livingston. If you don't know her, you don't understand YC." While her three co-founders were deeply technical, Jessica was the "Social Radar" — she read founders' character through tiny social cues. "Do the co-founders get along? Are these people committed? And if a founder would get defensive, that was always a bad sign." Every YC interview, everyone turned to her: "Jessica, what does the social radar say?" She's the one who noticed the Obama O's cereal boxes that convinced YC to fund Airbnb — even though Paul Graham hated the idea and tried to get Brian Chesky to change it. She also rejected founders from batches when their character was off: "Life's too short."

**Why It's a Hidden Gem:** Jessica rarely does interviews. She's the invisible co-founder of the most important startup institution in history. The story of the non-technical person whose human judgment built YC is deeply contrarian in a tech-obsessed world.

**Key Numbers:** Co-founded YC (which funded Airbnb, Stripe, Dropbox, etc.), rejected founders based on social cues alone
**Best Quote:** "My three co-founders were deeply technical, but I would look at other things about founders. All these little social cues."
**Emotional Core:** The power of reading people in a world obsessed with reading code. The invisible force behind Silicon Valley's greatest institution.

## 7. Matt MacInnis — "Years of Deluding Yourself About PMF"

**Source:** podcasts/matt-macinnis.md (2025-12-28)
**Guest:** Matt MacInnis (Rippling, formerly Inkling)

**The Story:** Matt spent years at Inkling convinced they had product-market fit. "Over and over again, over the course of the many years that I spent at Inkling, we thought we had it. We thought we had product market fit, maybe, maybe." Then he joined Rippling and experienced real PMF for the first time: "In hindsight, with the benefit of now having experienced solid product market fit, it was so, so obvious that we didn't." He doesn't regret the Inkling years — "I don't think the chapter I'm in right now could have come without the chapters before it" — but he's now acutely aware of mortality: "I got 20, 30, maybe if I'm lucky, 40 years left on the planet." His 10 contrarian truths include: "Never delude yourself into believing you have it when you don't. It is dangerous and regrettable."

**Why It's a Hidden Gem:** This isn't a victory lap — it's a confession. The brutal honesty of "I spent years lying to myself" is rare in tech. And the contrast between fake PMF and real PMF, felt in the bones, is deeply song-worthy.

**Key Numbers:** Years at Inkling with false PMF, invested in 60-70 companies, "20-40 years left on the planet"
**Best Quote:** "Product market fit is a sort of thing where you absolutely know it when you see it, and therefore if you don't absolutely know it, you don't have it."
**Emotional Core:** The devastating gap between hope and reality. The cost of self-deception measured in years.

## 8. Phyl Terry — "Never Search Alone"

**Source:** podcasts/phyl-terry.md (2024-09-12)
**Guest:** Phyl Terry (author of Never Search Alone)

**The Story:** Phyl's mom got laid off when he was young, and it scarred him. Then the dotcom bust hit and he watched hundreds of people lose their jobs. He built a framework — job search councils — where unemployed people support each other through the loneliest professional experience. The emotional core: "We're building a private safety net for all those who've been laid off or let go." In a world of layoffs and AI displacement, with net job losses in tech specifically, the episode addresses what Lenny's audience most fears but rarely talks about: What happens when you're the one who gets the email?

**Why It's a Hidden Gem:** Job search is taboo in tech — nobody wants to admit they're looking. But in the layoff era, this episode hits different. The song potential is in the universal fear of losing your job and the power of not searching alone.

**Key Numbers:** Net job losses in tech industry, job search councils helping hundreds
**Best Quote:** "We're building a private safety net for all those who've been laid off or let go."
**Emotional Core:** The loneliness of losing your job. The power of community in your darkest professional moment.

## 9. Kayvon Beykpour — "Fired on Paternity Leave" (Alternate Angle)

**Source:** podcasts/kayvon-beykpour.md (2024-04-28)
**Guest:** Kayvon Beykpour (ex-Head of Product, Twitter)

**Note:** This IS in deep-reads (#16), but the hidden gem angle is different. The deep-read focused on culture transformation. The hidden gem is the personal story: being fired from the company you transformed while on paternity leave, then walking back into Twitter HQ through the back door to meet Elon — "a very strange experience." He founded Periscope (which inspired Instagram Live, TikTok Live, Facebook Live), sold to Twitter, spent years transforming its stagnant culture, shipped Spaces/Super Follows/Communities — then got an email on pat leave. "Still no regrets. Had a lot of fun."

**Why It's a Hidden Gem (Alternate Angle):** The "fired on pat leave" detail is the most viscerally human moment in Lenny's archive. It's not about product strategy — it's about what happens when the company you gave everything to moves on without you. Song-worthy because everyone fears this.

**Key Numbers:** Founded Periscope, inspired 4+ competing live products, fired during paternity leave
**Best Quote:** "Still no regrets. Had a lot of fun."
**Emotional Core:** Grace under fire. Building something transformative and losing it while holding your newborn.

## 10. Carilu Dietrich — "Endurance Is the Secret"

**Source:** podcasts/carilu-dietrich.md (2023-04-30)
**Guest:** Carilu Dietrich (Atlassian IPO CMO, advisor to Miro, Segment, 1Password)

**The Story:** Carilu was the CMO who took Atlassian public — one of the most successful enterprise IPOs. But her deeper insight is about the long game: "The best executives I know have had down periods in their careers — where they were out of work for a while, where they were fired by a CEO. And what they have in common is endurance. They're just back in the ring." Her favorite interview question: "How many people have you fired? And tell me about each of the experiences." Because "if you've never fired anyone, then you haven't managed very many people for very long." And firing people is "the hardest part of a leader's career."

**Why It's a Hidden Gem:** Carilu isn't a flashy founder. She's a marketing executive who took a company public. But "endurance is the secret" is a thesis that contradicts the Silicon Valley myth of overnight success. The song potential is in the unglamorous truth: careers are long, setbacks are guaranteed, and the winners are the ones who keep getting back in the ring.

**Key Numbers:** Atlassian IPO, advisor to Miro/Segment/1Password, asks every hire about firing experiences
**Best Quote:** "The best executives I know have had down periods in their careers... What they have in common is endurance. They're just back in the ring."
**Emotional Core:** The unsexy truth about success: it's not brilliance, it's endurance. The courage to get back in the ring.
