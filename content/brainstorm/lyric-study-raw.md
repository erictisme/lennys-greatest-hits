# Lyric Study: Reference Artist Analysis

Research into 5 reference artists — what makes their lyrics work and what we can steal for Lenny's Greatest Hits.

---

## 1. Jon Guerra — Warmth, Honesty

**Style:** Devotional singer-songwriter. "Monday morning prayer music" — conversational, unhurried, intimate. Lyrics paired with space in the music; he doesn't cram songs with words.

### Song: "Let a Little Light In"
- **What works:** Shifts musically in its second half into a riff on Psalm 23:6. The lyric earns the shift — it builds from personal confession to communal hope.
- **Technique:** Questions posed in one song answered in the next. Creates an album-as-conversation feel.
- **Key line pattern:** Simple declarative sentences that land like prayers. No clever wordplay — just honest observation.

### Song: "Illness of the Heart"
- **What works:** Confessional repentance song that wrestles with sin in the vein of Psalm 51. Names the problem directly without dressing it up.
- **Technique:** Uses space — unhurried instrumentals let lyrics breathe. Words arrive like they cost something to say.

### Song: "One Thing I Have Asked"
- **What works:** Anthemic setting of Psalm 27:4. Takes ancient text and makes it feel like a personal letter.
- **Technique:** Repetition of the core phrase builds emotional weight without feeling lazy.

**Takeaways for our project:**
- Warmth comes from conversational tone, not clever rhymes
- Space in music lets lyrics land harder
- Repetition of a core phrase can be anthemic if earned
- Honest > clever every time

## 2. Jonathan Ogden — Vulnerability, Simplicity

**Style:** Manchester-based worship songwriter/producer. Deliberately strips production down to "let the lyrics shine through." Previously blended bedroom pop with hip-hop/electronic — chose simplicity as a conscious artistic decision.

### Song: "Keeper"
- **What works:** Lines about trust and divine care delivered with domestic intimacy. Feels like a conversation at a kitchen table, not a stage performance.
- **Technique:** Grounds writing in scripture but processes it through personal experience — dwells on a single text for days before writing.

### Song: "Garden"
- **What works:** Uses natural imagery (growth, seasons) as emotional metaphor without being heavy-handed.
- **Technique:** Short lines. Lets single images carry whole verses. The listener fills in the gaps.

### Song: "You Bought Me Flowers" (from Substack writing)
- **What works:** "I didn't hear your voice drifting through from the other room" — the specificity of "from the other room" makes it feel lived-in.
- **Technique:** Took nearly two years to develop a song about suffering because he needed time to "process those feelings" and find "the language to what I was feeling."

**Takeaways for our project:**
- Simplicity is a choice, not a limitation — strip production to let lyrics breathe
- Specific domestic details ("from the other room") beat grand abstractions
- Don't rush emotional content — find the right language, not the first language
- Living "with antenna up" — stay receptive to inspiration from unexpected sources

## 3. Bon Iver / Justin Vernon — Abstract Imagery, Emotional Weight

**Style:** Builds emotional landscapes through fragmented, oblique lyrics. Words chosen as much for their sound as their meaning. Created iconic album "For Emma, Forever Ago" in isolation in a Wisconsin cabin.

### Song: "Skinny Love"
- **What works:** The phrase "skinny love" itself — creates a new compound metaphor. Love that's underfed, malnourished, running on fumes. "Come on skinny love, just last the year" — desperation in a whisper.
- **Technique:** Invented vocabulary. "Skinny love" didn't exist as a phrase before this song. It's now in the cultural lexicon.
- **Line length:** Short, punchy. Stacked images, not complete sentences.

### Song: "Holocene"
- **What works:** "And at once I knew I was not magnificent" — existential humility in 9 words. Emerged during an icy night walk. Captures the moment ego dissolves in nature.
- **Technique:** One devastating line surrounded by atmospheric fragments. The clarity of that single line hits harder because everything around it is impressionistic.
- **Vowel sounds:** Long open vowels ("once," "knew," "not") that let the voice stretch and ache.

### Song: "Re: Stacks"
- **What works:** "This is not the sound of a new man or a crispy realization" — refuses the easy redemption arc. Sits in the mess. "Your love will be safe with me" closes with a quiet promise after all the heaviness.
- **Technique:** Negation as a lyric tool — saying what something ISN'T to make the listener feel what it IS. Gambling metaphors (stacks = poker chips) woven through without explanation.
- **Themes:** Loss, resilience, reflection, healing — but never names them directly.

### Song: "For Emma"
- **What works:** "With all your lies, you're still very loveable" — captures contradictory feelings of loving someone destructive. Vernon says Emma represents emotional pain, not a person.
- **Technique:** Personification of abstract concepts. Addressing "Emma" as a character gives pain a face.

**Takeaways for our project:**
- Invent vocabulary when existing words aren't enough
- One crystal-clear line surrounded by atmosphere > all clear lines
- Negation ("this is not...") creates powerful emotional contrast
- Vowel sounds matter for singability — long open vowels let voices stretch
- Abstract imagery earns emotional weight when grounded in specific sensory detail (icy nights, poker chips)

## 4. Phoebe Bridgers — Dry Wit, Specificity

**Style:** Unflinching emotional honesty delivered through hyper-specific details and dark humor. Juxtaposes the mundane with the devastating. Structure serves lyrics, not the other way around.

### Song: "Motion Sickness"
- **What works:** "I hate you for what you did / And I miss you like a little kid" — the childlike simile cuts through the adult anger. "I have emotional motion sickness / Somebody roll the windows down" — transforms an abstract feeling into a physical, relatable sensation.
- **Technique:** Varies chorus lyrics across 4 repetitions — first two identical, third completely different, fourth keeps only the title line. Verse lengths are uneven (13 bars, then 11) because lyrics shape structure, not the other way around.
- **Specificity:** References a real ex who "sings in a peculiar British accent" and gave her "money to see a hypnotherapist." These absurdly specific details make the universal feelings hit harder.

### Song: "Funeral"
- **What works:** "Jesus Christ, I'm so blue all the time / And that's just how I feel / Always have" — the resigned simplicity of "always have" after the dramatic opening. No redemption arc, no silver lining.
- **Technique:** The song is about a friend's overdose, but she toggles between their death and her own depression — the guilt of making someone else's tragedy about yourself IS the emotional truth.
- **Specificity:** Real death, real depression, real self-awareness about being self-absorbed in grief.

### Song: "Kyoto"
- **What works:** Captures complicated feelings about her father. Uses the disconnect between being in a beautiful place (Kyoto) and feeling emotionally terrible.
- **Technique:** Geographic specificity as emotional contrast — naming a real place grounds abstract feelings.

### Song: "Garden Song"
- **What works:** "The doctor put her hands over my liver / She told me my resentment's getting smaller" — medical imagery for emotional healing. The liver as the organ that processes poison = resentment.
- **Technique:** Unexpected metaphor vehicles — doctors, livers, physical examinations as stand-ins for emotional inventory.

**Takeaways for our project:**
- Hyper-specific details (real places, real people, real objects) make universal feelings hit harder
- Dry humor and emotional devastation in the same line = distinctive voice
- Let lyrics dictate song structure — uneven bar counts feel more organic
- Vary your choruses — don't repeat the same words 4 times
- The mundane (motion sickness, doctor visits) as metaphor for the profound
- Self-awareness about your own emotional responses IS the emotional content

## 5. Iron and Wine (Sam Beam) — Pastoral Imagery, Storytelling

**Style:** Oblique narrative approach full of "realistic and evocative visual detail." Started as a poet, converted poetry to lyrics. Prioritizes how words SOUND when sung — treats sonic quality of words as "deeply important."

### Song: "Flightless Bird, American Mouth"
- **What works:** "Diving too deep for coins" — originally "my candy coins" but Beam changed it because he "liked the idea of reflecting on yourself as someone diving too deep for things you were after." Every revision made the image more universal.
- **Technique:** Cinematic expansion — starts with specific childhood detail, zooms out to American identity. Beam says it's "me talking to America and describing our relationship."
- **Sound craft:** Opening lines use similar consonant patterns. The "ir" sound recurs at verse endings. Words are chosen for mouth-feel as much as meaning.

### Song: "Naked As We Came"
- **What works:** Intimate look at love stripped of pretense. Evokes Nick Drake's stripped-down beauty. The title itself is the thesis — we arrive and leave with nothing.
- **Technique:** Pastoral imagery (nature, bodies, seasons) as stand-ins for the human condition. Minimal words, maximum resonance.

### Song: "Boy With a Coin"
- **What works:** Hypnotic, rhythmic gallop. Juxtaposes innocent objects (coins, birds) with dark circumstances.
- **Technique:** Phonic patterning — "boy/coin, girl/bird" use similar consonant structures. The "ir" sound creates cohesion. Symbolic imagery provokes interpretation rather than providing answers.
- **Sound devices:** Trance-like grooves with limited vocal sections means every word must earn its place.

**Takeaways for our project:**
- Prioritize how words SOUND when sung — consonant patterns, vowel sounds, mouth-feel
- Revise toward the more universal image (coins > candy coins)
- Cinematic zoom: start specific, pull back to big theme
- Juxtapose innocent/playful imagery with darker themes
- Every word must earn its place when vocal sections are limited
- Symbolic, open-ended imagery > explicit explanation — let listeners connect the dots

## Cross-Artist Patterns

| Technique | Artists Who Use It | Priority for Us |
|---|---|---|
| Specificity beats abstraction | Bridgers, Ogden, Beam | HIGH — our batch 1 lyrics were too generic |
| Sound of words matters as much as meaning | Vernon, Beam | HIGH — Suno needs singable vowels |
| Space and silence let lyrics land | Guerra, Ogden | MEDIUM — depends on genre |
| Vary chorus lyrics across repetitions | Bridgers | HIGH — avoids repetitive AI feel |
| One clear line amid atmosphere | Vernon | HIGH — gives each song a quotable moment |
| Dry humor + emotional weight | Bridgers | MEDIUM — fits some genres better |
| Negation as technique | Vernon | LOW — use sparingly |
| Pastoral/nature imagery | Beam, Ogden | MEDIUM — fits folk/acoustic tracks |
| Invented vocabulary | Vernon | LOW — risky with Suno pronunciation |
| Revision toward universality | Beam | HIGH — always choose the more resonant image |
