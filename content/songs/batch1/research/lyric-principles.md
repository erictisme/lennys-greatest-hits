# Lyric Craft Principles

Extracted from reference artist study + batch 1 audit. Each principle has a bad example (from batch 1 or generic writing) and a good example (from reference artists or rewrites).

---

## 1. Specificity Beats Abstraction

Concrete details make universal feelings hit harder. Name the real thing — the place, the object, the number.

- **Bad:** "Analyzed the data, nothing we had missed" (Talk to Users — vague, tells us nothing visual)
- **Good:** "I didn't hear your voice drifting through from the other room" (Ogden — the room makes it lived-in)

---

## 2. Show, Don't Tell

Let images carry meaning instead of stating the lesson directly. The listener should feel the truth before you name it.

- **Bad:** "Burnout isn't working too much / It's working on the wrong things" (Burnout — defines the concept like a blog post)
- **Good:** "I have emotional motion sickness / Somebody roll the windows down" (Bridgers — transforms an abstract feeling into a physical sensation you can feel in your body)

---

## 3. Syllable Economy — Every Word Earns Its Place

Short lines hit harder. If you can cut a word without losing meaning, cut it. Aim for 6-10 syllables per line in verses, allow more in choruses for melodic stretch.

- **Bad:** "Color-coded dashboards, segments, cohort views / But somehow every launch still missed the cues" (Talk to Users — 12+ syllables, stuffed with jargon)
- **Good:** "Come on skinny love, just last the year" (Vernon — 9 syllables, every word carries emotional weight)

---

## 4. Vowel Sounds Matter for Singability

Long open vowels (oh, oo, ah, ay) let voices stretch and ache. Closed consonant clusters (sts, nts, cts) choke singers. Write for the mouth, not the page.

- **Bad:** "Continuous discovery, week by week" (Talk to Users — "continuous discovery" is a mouthful of closed syllables)
- **Good:** "And at once I knew I was not magnificent" (Vernon — long vowels on "once," "knew," "not" let the voice open up)

---

## 5. One Killer Line Per Song

Every song needs one line so clear and resonant that listeners remember it after one play. Surround it with atmosphere — not every line needs to be a thesis statement.

- **Bad:** "The best PMs ship — the rest make decks for free" (Ship It — tries to make EVERY line quotable, nothing stands out)
- **Good:** "This is not the sound of a new man or a crispy realization" (Vernon — one devastating line amid impressionistic fragments, impossible to forget)

---

## 6. Vary Your Choruses

Repeating identical chorus lyrics 3-4 times creates an AI/karaoke feel. Change a word, swap a line, let the chorus evolve with the story.

- **Bad:** All 10 batch 1 songs repeat their choruses word-for-word every time
- **Good:** Bridgers varies "Motion Sickness" chorus across 4 repetitions — first two identical, third completely different, fourth keeps only the title line

---

## 7. Rhyme Quality Over Rhyme Quantity

Forced perfect rhymes sound like greeting cards. Slant rhymes and near-rhymes feel more natural and conversational. When a rhyme feels forced, break it.

- **Bad:** "Gleaming on the surface, sinking for years" forced to rhyme with "ideas" (The Maze — stretches the rhyme)
- **Good:** Guerra's conversational delivery — honest observation over clever wordplay, rhymes only when they arrive naturally

---

## 8. Let Lyrics Shape Structure

Don't force lyrics into rigid 4-bar, 4-bar patterns. If a verse needs 5 lines, give it 5. If a bridge wants to be two lines, let it. Organic structure signals authenticity.

- **Bad:** "I packed a camera and a carry-on / Knocked on every door from dusk to dawn" (Do Things — metronomic couplets that feel mechanical)
- **Good:** Bridgers writes uneven verse lengths (13 bars, then 11) because lyrics shape structure, not the other way around

---

## 9. Humor and Emotional Weight in the Same Breath

The contrast between wit and sincerity creates a distinctive voice. Pure earnestness risks feeling preachy; pure humor risks feeling shallow.

- **Bad:** "If you're not embarrassed, you waited too long to leave" (Ship It — quote delivered straight, no tonal contrast)
- **Good:** "I hate you for what you did / And I miss you like a little kid" (Bridgers — the childlike simile cuts through adult anger)

---

## 10. Revision Toward the Universal

First drafts are specific to you. Revise toward images anyone can feel. Cut insider jargon unless the jargon IS the point (like "vibe coding").

- **Bad:** "Feather, brick, then dump truck — can't deny" (Burnout — Lenny-specific reference that means nothing without context)
- **Good:** Beam changed "my candy coins" to "coins" — "I liked the idea of reflecting on yourself as someone diving too deep for things you were after." More universal, more resonant.
