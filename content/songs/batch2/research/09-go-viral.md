## Story Arc

Nikita Bier spent 4-5 years building 15 consumer apps — 14 of them duds. His company Midnight Labs was on its last legs. His key engineer had put in two weeks' notice. He called his lawyer to ask how to dissolve a company. Then he noticed something insane: the #1 app in the United States was called Sarahah, and it was entirely in Arabic. That was "the strongest signal that you could ever have that people want something" — latent demand for anonymous social feedback. But instead of copying the anonymous hate angle, he flipped it: what if you could only say positive things? He built TBH — a compliment polling app — in weeks.

They launched at a single high school in Georgia (picked because it had the earliest school start date in the US). 40% of the school downloaded it in 24 hours. One school sent 450,000 messages in 7 days. Users were sending 60 messages per day when normal was 3-4. Servers crashed. The Amazon bill hit $120K against $150K in the bank. They geofenced the app state by state to control growth, rolling it out like a wildfire they chose to feed. At peak: 360,000 downloads per day. The whole team was 2 engineers, 1 designer, and Nikita.

Nine weeks after launch, he sold TBH to Facebook for over $30 million. Facebook sent 80 people for the deal. Nikita had one. They visited his dingy $1,800/month Oakland office and said: "This is the whole company? This is the number one app in the United States?" After 4 years at Facebook, he left and did it again with Gas — same school, same day, 5 years later. Gas hit $11 million in revenue and 10 million downloads. He survived a fabricated human trafficking hoax that nearly killed the company, fought it on every front, and sold to Discord. The man has built two separate #1 apps in the US App Store with skeleton crews. His secret: find what people desperately want but can barely get, then make it effortless.

## Emotional Core

This is a story about the addictive, electric feeling of watching something you built spread across an entire country in real time — and the grueling, sleep-deprived, nearly-broke reality underneath the magic. It captures the founder's paradox: the day before becoming #1 in the country, you're calling your lawyer about how to shut down. It's about persistence through 14 failures, about a 4-person team in a cheap Oakland office outrunning 80-person Facebook deal teams, about the "thrilling drug" of virality that makes you do it all over again five years later. The human tension is between the glamour of going viral and the unglamorous truth of what it costs — 3 hours of sleep, $120K Amazon bills against $150K in the bank, fighting fabricated hoaxes school superintendent by school superintendent. It's exhilarating and exhausting in equal measure.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

If you find what people desperately want but can barely get, and you make it effortless — it will spread like wildfire, and no amount of money or headcount can compete with that kind of product-market fit.
