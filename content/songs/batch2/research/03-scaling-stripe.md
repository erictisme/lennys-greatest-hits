## Story Arc

Claire Hughes Johnson spent almost 11 years at Google, joining at 1,800 people pre-IPO and riding it to 60,000. She had 8 different jobs there. Then she jumped to Stripe when it was just 160 people — and immediately did the math on support tickets, sales leads, and compliance needs. Her conclusion: "This company should be probably twice the number of people it is right now." Which, of course, freaked everybody out.

Over the next 7 years as COO, she helped scale Stripe from 160 to over 7,000 people. But the real story isn't the headcount — it's the *infrastructure* she built to keep the humans working together. Founding documents. Operating principles. A culture of writing things down. Levels and ladders implemented early (she called Square and Airbnb first — one said "that's a bloodbath," the other said "I'm impressed you're doing it this early, we waited until 800 people and it was horrible"). Flat titles where expertise mattered more than hierarchy. A "spin the wheel" metrics review where random teams present their dashboard live — no prep allowed.

The turning point is Claire's core thesis: product-market fit is just a product — it is NOT a company, and it will not scale. Someone has to build the machine that builds the product. That's the unglamorous, deeply human work of operating at scale. John Collison captured it perfectly: customers kept asking how Stripe scaled, and he'd say "talk to Claire." Eventually he joked, "We need Claire in a box." The book *Scaling People* became that box.

## Emotional Core

This is a song about the invisible architect. Everyone celebrates the founders, the product, the billion-dollar valuation. But someone had to build the machine that made it all possible — the operating docs, the hiring loops, the hard conversations about levels and titles that nobody wants to have. Claire is the person in the room saying the thing everyone is thinking but won't say. There's a deep tension between the messiness of humans and the need for structure. "It would be easy to build companies if there weren't humans involved." The emotion is pride mixed with exhaustion — the operator who keeps the engine running while everyone else takes a bow. It's also about not getting stuck: the courage to decide, to act, to be a force for momentum even when you're unsure.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

When everything's growing fast and chaos is everywhere, the decision-maker is probably you — don't get stuck, build the machine, be a force for momentum.
