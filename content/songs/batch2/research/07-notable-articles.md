## 1. The Inside Story of Facebook Marketplace
**Source:** `newsletters/the-inside-story-of-facebook-marketplace.md` — Deb Liu
**Topic:** Marketplace building

### Core Framework
1. Find existing behavior to amplify (Facebook Groups already had buying/selling)
2. Solve trust first (real identities = built-in trust layer)
3. Seed supply by cross-posting from existing communities
4. Measure weekly buyer retention as the north star metric
5. Iterate relentlessly on discovery and matching

### Company Examples with Numbers
- Facebook Marketplace reached **1 billion+ MAU**
- Cross-posted listings from Facebook Groups to seed initial supply
- Took **5 years** from idea to massive scale — "five-year overnight success"
- Deb Liu pitched the idea internally multiple times before getting greenlit

### The ONE Insight
Marketplaces don't need to create new behavior — they need to find where the behavior already exists and make it 10x easier. Facebook didn't invent buying/selling; they paved the cowpath from Groups.

### Best Quote
> "It was a five-year overnight success."

## 2. Pricing Your SaaS Product
**Source:** `newsletters/pricing-your-saas-product.md` — Patrick Campbell (ProfitWell)
**Topic:** SaaS pricing strategy

### Core Framework
1. Identify your **value metric** (the unit customers pay for)
2. Segment customers by willingness to pay
3. Localize pricing by currency (not just language)
4. Experiment quarterly — most companies don't touch pricing for years
5. Avoid discounting over 20%

### Company Examples with Numbers
- Companies with **currency localization** see **30% higher revenue per customer**
- Discounting over **20%** correlates with **higher churn rates**
- Most successful companies optimize monetization **every quarter**
- ProfitWell analyzed **thousands** of SaaS companies to find these patterns

### The ONE Insight
Pricing is not a one-time decision — it's a living system. The companies that win treat pricing like a product feature they iterate on quarterly, not a number they set at launch and forget.

### Best Quote
> "Most successful companies optimize monetization every quarter."

## 5. A Playbook for Fundraising
**Source:** `newsletters/a-playbook-for-fundraising.md` — Marc McCabe
**Topic:** Startup fundraising

### Core Framework (4 steps)
1. **Preparation** (4 weeks): Build deck, narrative, data room
2. **Outreach** (2 weeks): Build list of 50-60 funds, warm intros
3. **Navigate the process** (2-3 weeks): Coordinate meetings in tight window
4. **Partner meetings + closing** (1-2 weeks): Create urgency, negotiate terms

### Company Examples with Numbers
- Start fundraising with **8+ months of runway** remaining
- Build a target list of **50-60 funds**
- Spend **4 weeks** building your deck before any outreach
- Coordinate all outreach in a **2-3 week window** to create FOMO
- Total process: **~10 weeks** from start to close

### The ONE Insight
Fundraising is a sales process, not a relationship process. The companies that raise fastest treat it like a compressed sprint — coordinating timing to create competitive pressure among VCs, not slowly building relationships over months.

### Best Quote
> "Start fundraising with at least 8 months of runway."

## 11. Freemium vs. Trial
**Source:** `newsletters/freemium-vs-trial.md`
**Topic:** Pricing and acquisition strategy

### Core Framework
1. Free is an **acquisition strategy**, not a pricing strategy
2. 90% of freemium products also offer a trial of premium features
3. Choose trial when: high price point + product needs hand-holding
4. Choose freemium when: low price point + self-serve + network effects
5. Many companies use both simultaneously

### Company Examples with Numbers
- **90%** of freemium products also offer a trial
- Freemium works best with **low price points** and **self-serve** products
- Trial works best with **high price points** and **complex** products
- Most successful companies use a **hybrid** approach

### The ONE Insight
The freemium vs. trial debate is a false dichotomy. Almost everyone does both. The real question isn't "which one?" — it's "what's free, what's trialed, and what's paid?" Free is your marketing channel, not your business model.

### Best Quote
> "Free isn't a pricing strategy — it's an acquisition strategy."

## 12. Five Steps to Starting Your Product-Led Growth Motion
**Source:** `newsletters/five-steps-to-starting-your-product-led-growth-motion.md` — Hila Qu
**Topic:** Product-led growth implementation

### Core Framework (5 steps)
1. **Map the full funnel** — PLG funnel looks different from SLG funnel
2. **Pick the starting point** — don't try to PLG everything at once
3. **Anticipate pitfalls** — cannibalization fears, team resistance
4. **Set up infrastructure** — analytics, experimentation, self-serve billing
5. **Build the team** — growth team ≠ product team

### Company Examples with Numbers
- PLG companies have **multiple aha moments**: user-level, team-level, buyer-level, paid-customer-level
- PLG funnel has fundamentally **different stages** than sales-led funnel
- Most companies fail at PLG because they try to **convert the entire funnel** at once
- Successful PLG starts with **one segment** or **one part of the funnel**

### The ONE Insight
PLG isn't a strategy you "turn on" — it's a second funnel you build alongside your existing one. Companies fail at PLG because they treat it as a feature (add a free plan!) instead of an entirely new go-to-market motion that needs its own team, metrics, and infrastructure.

### Best Quote
> "PLG has multiple aha moments: user, team, buyer, and paid-customer."

## 16. How Perplexity Builds Product
**Source:** `newsletters/how-perplexity-builds-product.md`
**Topic:** AI-first company building

### Core Framework
1. Tiny teams of **2-3 people** per project
2. Minimal PMs — **2 PMs for 50 people**
3. Organize like "slime mold" — minimize coordination overhead
4. **75% of goals are weekly**, not quarterly
5. Hackathons as the primary mechanism for new feature development

### Company Examples with Numbers
- **Perplexity**: **2 PMs** for **50 people** — radically low PM-to-engineer ratio
- Teams of **2-3 people** tackle projects end-to-end
- **75%** of goals are set **weekly** — not quarterly OKRs
- Hackathons generate new features — shipping speed is the culture
- Organized like **"slime mold"** — no rigid hierarchy, teams form around problems

### The ONE Insight
The future of product development isn't better processes — it's fewer processes. Perplexity's radical organizational model (2 PMs for 50 people, weekly goals, teams of 2-3) isn't chaotic — it's what happens when you trust engineers to make product decisions and remove every layer that slows them down.

### Best Quote
> "We organize like slime mold — to minimize coordination."
