## 1. "Wartime" — Eoghan McCabe / Intercom

**Source:** podcasts/eoghan-mccabe.md (13,585 words, Aug 2025)

### Story Arc

**Beginning (The Crisis):** Intercom, 14 years old and worth multiple billions, hit a wall. Eoghan had been sick (mold toxins + tick bite) and left the CEO role in 2020. During his absence, the company became bloated, unfocused — "trying to do all the things for all the people." Revenue growth cratered to low single-digit percentages. Then came five consecutive quarters of sequential decline in net new ARR, heading toward $0 — which would have meant negative growth territory. Pricing became a public meme; people on Twitter made viral jokes about how expensive Intercom was.

**Middle (ChatGPT & Wartime):** Eoghan came back as CEO. One month later, ChatGPT launched. Six weeks after GPT-3.5 dropped, they had a working beta of what would become Fin. Des Traynor (co-founder) texted Eoghan: "the AI team have something interesting and they actually think we could make a product out of this." Eoghan declared Intercom a "wartime company." He cut costs aggressively, killed projects, froze the office fit-out. He picked a lane — customer service only — abandoning ~$80M ARR from other products. Announced ~$100M of own cash would be spent on AI. Rewrote company values as "a sharp knife" to cut people who wouldn't be effective. Implemented a hard-coded quarterly formula where employees scoring below a threshold were let go. ~40% employee turnover. Letters sent to the board. A "soft coup" attempt. Glassdoor CEO rating hit historic lows.

**End (Fin's Triumph):** Fin grew from $1M to $12M ARR in year one, then into "solid mid-eight digit ARR." On track to pass $100M ARR in less than three quarters — growing north of 300%. Intercom now in the 15th percentile of all ~120 public B2B software companies by ARR growth. #1 by customer count, revenue, and performance in the CX AI agent category. 15-16 months after the culture rebuild, anonymous employee survey showed 98-99% approval — a complete reversal from the Glassdoor lows.

### Every Specific Number

- 5 consecutive quarters of sequential decline in net new ARR
- Multiple hundreds of millions in ARR at time of pivot
- $80M ARR abandoned from non-service products
- ~$100M of own cash committed to AI
- $50M in ARR given away in pricing simplification
- $0.99 per resolved ticket — Fin's outcome-based pricing
- 120 cents cost per ticket at launch (losing money per transaction)
- $20-$30 per ticket industry average; Intercom customers ~$22

- 30,000 paying customers when Fin launched
- Fin Year 1: $1M → $12M ARR
- Fin now: will pass $100M ARR in < 3 quarters, growing 300%+
- 6 weeks from GPT-3.5 launch to working Fin beta
- 98-99% employee approval in anonymous survey (15-16 months post-rebuild)

## 2. "Streak" — Duolingo's Gamification Growth Story

**Source:** newsletters/how-duolingo-reignited-user-growth.md (4,812 words, Feb 2023)

### Story Arc

**Beginning (Stalled Growth):** Jorge Mazal joined as Head of Product in late 2017. By mid-2018, DAU was growing at single-digit rate YoY. The company needed a growth breakthrough.

**Middle (Two Failures, Then Insight):** First attempt: copied Gardenscapes' moves counter gamification mechanic. Months of work. Result: completely neutral. "Depressingly, the result of all that effort was completely neutral. I was deflated." Team "almost immediately and dramatically disbanded." Second attempt: referral program inspired by Uber. Only 3% increase in new users. Then Jorge regrouped. Built a MECE user segmentation model (adapted from Zynga and MyFitnessPal). Discovered CURR (current user retention rate) had 5x the impact on DAU of any other metric. Created a dedicated Retention Team with CURR as North Star.

**End (4.5x Growth):** Three vectors drove the breakthrough:
1. **Leaderboards** — borrowed from Zynga's FarmVille 2 but adapted (auto opt-in, no extra tasks). Learning time up 17%, highly engaged learners tripled.
2. **Push notifications** — "protect the channel" principle learned from Groupon CEO cautionary tale. Countless small wins accumulated into substantial DAU gains year over year.
3. **Streaks** — APM discovered users who reached 10-day streak had substantially lower drop-off. Streak-saver notification was first big win. Calendar views, animations, rewards followed.

Result: CURR up 21%, daily churn of best users reduced 40%+, DAU grew 4.5x over four years. Share of DAU with 7+ day streak nearly tripled. Supercharged IPO.

### Every Specific Number

- DAU growing at single-digit rate YoY in mid-2018
- First gamification experiment: completely neutral result
- Referral program: only 3% increase in new users
- CURR had 5x the impact on DAU of any other metric
- Leaderboards: 17% increase in overall learning time, highly engaged learners tripled
- CURR improved by 21% over four years
- Daily churn of best users reduced by 40%+
- DAU grew 4.5x over four years
- 7+ day streak users grew to more than half of DAU (nearly 3x increase)
- One user: 1,435-day streak with no streak freezes

### Quotable Lines

- "Depressingly, the result of all that effort was completely neutral. No change to our retention. No increase in DAU. We hardly got any user feedback at all. I was deflated."
- "The team almost immediately (and dramatically) disbanded, and the idea was abandoned. It was pretty awful."
- "I had been so focused on the similarities between Gardenscapes and Duolingo that I had failed to account for the importance of the underlying differences."
- "Protect the channel." (foundational rule for push notifications)
- "One often underappreciated risk with aggressively A/B testing emails and push notifications is that it results in users opting out of the channel; and even if you kill the test, those users remain opted out forever."
- "I have a 1,435-day streak! With no streak freezes!"
- "Each day that a learner comes to Duolingo, they care a bit more about coming back the next day than they did the day before."

### Emotional Turning Point

The moment the moves counter experiment returned completely neutral results and the team disbanded. The failure forced a period of deep reflection that led to the entire data-driven growth model. The secondary turning point: seeing CURR's 5x impact in the sensitivity analysis — the "aha" that changed everything.

## 4. "Complicit" — Jerry Colonna

**Source:** podcasts/jerry-colonna.md (11,633 words, May 2025)

### Story Arc

**Beginning (VC Career):** Co-founded Flatiron Partners with Fred Wilson — one of the most successful early-stage investment funds ever. Also a partner at JPMorgan Chase. VC for 15-17 years.

**Middle (Crisis & Transition):** In his late 30s, despite enormous outward success, depression that had marked most of his life became so severe he was suicidal and lost. This crisis became the seed for his coaching career. Founded Reboot with co-founder Ali Schultz, built on the belief that "better humans make better leaders."

**End (The Framework):** Now ~27 years coaching. Developed the "complicit" question and the full 4-question radical self-inquiry toolkit. Author of two books (Reboot, Reunion), working on a third. Turning 62, asking: "What do I want my elderhood to be like?"

### The "Complicit" Framework — Full Detail

**The question:** "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?"

**Why "complicit" not "responsible":** Complicit = accomplice, not perpetrator. "You are driving the getaway car, you're not sticking up the bank teller." Not about blame — about noticing participation.

**Worked example (busyness):** "I say I don't want to feel busy all the time, but the truth is I feel really unnerved and disconcerted if my agenda isn't jam-packed."

**Delegation example (15-person CEO):** She says nobody can make decisions without her. Jerry: "Who hired them?" / "How does it make you feel when they make a decision you disagree with?" / "I'm furious." — She's complicit.

**Full 4-question suite:**
1. How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?
2. What am I not saying that I need to say?
3. What am I saying that's not being heard?
4. What's being said that I'm not hearing?

### Every Specific Number

- 15-17 years as a VC
- 27 years coaching
- Late 30s when suicidal crisis hit
- 25 years — Bruce Springsteen spent in psychoanalysis
- 62 years old at time of recording
- 15-person company in the delegation example

### Quotable Lines

- "How have I been complicit in creating the conditions I say I don't want?"
- "To understand the word complicit, think of the word accomplice. You are driving the getaway car, you're not sticking up the bank teller."

- "I get you want to be a great CEO, I get you want to be a great executive, but what I really care about is you not killing yourself in the process."
- "It's not only not the case [that success brings happiness], it's the big lie that we're socialized with since childhood."

### Emotional Turning Point

The moment Jerry reveals his own suicidal crisis while explaining the equation on the whiteboard: "I get you want to be a great CEO... but what I really care about is you not killing yourself in the process." The entire framework comes from a man who had everything externally and was falling apart internally.

## 5. "Freemium Killed Us" — Bobby Pinero / Equals

**Source:** newsletters/lessons-from-going-freemium-a-decision-that-broke-our-business.md (3,446 words, Nov 2023)

### Story Arc

**Beginning (High Friction Success):** Launched Equals (next-gen spreadsheet) in April 2022. From day one: onboarding call required, pay upfront, no trial, connect a data source. Maximum friction. Revenue took off. Raised $16M Series A from a16z in five months.

**Middle (The Freemium Experiment):** Customers screamed for self-serve and free plans. Advisors agreed. They caved:
- Announced Series A with free plan, no onboarding call, slashed prices
- Still required data source connection
- Immediately 4x'd weekly active companies
- Then... stalled. Couldn't grow active company base.
- Tried allowing CSV upload instead of live data source → hurt engagement further
- Tried letting users skip data source entirely → "We blew up. Thousands of active companies. Just kidding. It was awful."
- Each attempt to reduce friction made things worse

**End (The Reversal):** Killed free plan. Added friction back: 14-day free trial with credit card required, data source connection required. ARR and customers immediately bounced back. Number of deeply engaged users went UP. Same number of new signups, but now converting and activating where before they would have dropped off.

### Every Specific Number

- $16M Series A from a16z (within 5 months of launch)
- 4x'd active companies initially after going freemium
- Then stalled and couldn't grow
- Each friction-reduction experiment made things worse
- After reversal: ARR and deeply engaged users immediately recovered

### Quotable Lines

- "We blew up. Thousands and thousands of active companies using Equals. Just kidding. It was awful."
- "We found two real ways to build and maintain momentum."
- "The goal of onboarding is not for people to complete onboarding. It's for people to get their first moments of value from your product."
- "Your users may tell you otherwise, but this is why adding friction to onboarding can outperform."
- "The allure of seeing a new product is the strongest motivator new users have to complete setup tasks."
- "The customer can be wrong."
- "If you make onboarding too easy, they'll never come back to do the hard task you let them skip."
- "The act of putting in their credit card increases their level of commitment to go through the hard steps. A free plan does the opposite."
- "I'm glad we tried freemium, despite all the challenges and existential dread."

### Emotional Turning Point

The "just kidding, it was awful" moment — when they bypassed all friction and expected thousands of engaged users, only to watch engagement and retention crater. The secondary punch: realizing they couldn't raise a meaningful Series B with stalled growth, which gave them "the evidence we needed to act with conviction."

### Connections to Other Lenny Episodes

- Elena Verna and Patrick Campbell gave feedback on early drafts
- The article is Bobby's first-person essay published in Lenny's newsletter
- References Notion, Figma, Airtable, Canva, Miro, Loom as the "darlings of SaaS" freemium model

## 7. "Give Away Your Legos" — Molly Graham

**Source:** podcasts/molly-graham.md (~98K chars, date not specified)

### Story Arc

**Beginning (Google → Facebook):** Joined Google the week the iPhone launched (2007). Comms dept: 25 people → 125 in 9 months. Followed Sheryl Sandberg and Elliot Schrage to Facebook in 2008 when it had 80M users (smaller than MySpace), $270M revenue, 500 employees. Left after IPO: 5,500 employees, $5B revenue, 1B+ users. The much more fun careers are like jumping off cliffs — you do fall, but then you climb out way beyond where the stairs could ever get you."

If you just stay and build houses, "eventually you're literally buried under a pile of Legos."

## 8. "When Enough Is Enough" — Andy Johns

**Source:** podcasts/andy-johns.md (12,718 words, Sep 2023)

### Story Arc

**Beginning (The Rise):** 17 years in startups across 6 consecutive companies. Product and growth leader at Facebook, Twitter, Quora, Wealthfront (VP Growth → VP Product → President). Was next in line to become CEO of Wealthfront. Making "high six figures per year into early seven figures."

**Root Cause (Childhood):** Mother was severely mentally ill (bipolar, psychosis, in and out of psychiatric hospitals). She died when Andy was 10. He discovered that achievement brought love and validation. "I learned very early on that if I wanted to feel good, I needed to achieve."

**Middle (Crisis at the Peak):** At Twitter in 2010, late 20s. Near-constant panic attacks and depression. Would grab his laptop, throw it in his bag, quietly walk out of the office — sometimes at 10 AM after only an hour. Scheduled to speak at company all-hands but had to bail. ## 10. "No KPIs" — Archie Abrams / Shopify

**Source:** podcasts/archie-abrams.md (13,940 words, Nov 2024)

### Story Arc

**The Structure:** Shopify has three product groups with fundamentally different philosophies:
- **Core Product:** No KPIs. No OKRs. Driven by taste and technical architecture quality. Led by Glen under Tobi. Builds for "commerce 100 years from now."
- **Merchant Services:** Payments, shipping. Medium-term horizon.
- **Growth (Archie's org):** 600+ people. Has metrics, but measures cohort GMV over 3-5 years, not conversion rates.

**The Churn Paradox:** Most SaaS companies dread churn. Shopify deliberately lowers barriers, expecting churn. Revenue model is tied to GMV (payments), not subscriptions. Like an angel investor: most bets fail, but winners (Allbirds, FIGS) make the whole cohort profitable. The $39/month subscription is almost incidental.

**The Long-Holdout System:** Every experiment watched at 3, 6, 9, and 12 months. Found that 30-40% of experiments showing short-term lift have ZERO long-term GMV lift — just "pull-forward" effects. The payment failure notification experiment looked like a massive win short-term, but zero long-term GMV lift. Why? "People who were letting that payment fail probably weren't actually that dedicated to this entrepreneurship craft."

**The 100-Year Vision:** Tobi's orientation. Big brands today will be gone in 30-50 years. Only companies that started on Shopify create real long-term value. Every R&D group lead meets with Tobi every 6 weeks to review every single project.
