## Story Arc

Every startup faces the same terrifying question: how do you get your first users when nobody knows you exist? Lenny spent a month personally reaching out to founders, scouring interviews, and tapping Twitter to compile the definitive answer. What he found was surprisingly simple: just seven strategies account for every major consumer app's early growth. And most companies only needed ONE.

The stories are legendary. Whitney Wolfe and Justin Mateen running around USC pitching Tinder to sororities and fraternities. DoorDash starting as "paloaltodelivery.com" with PDF menus and $6 delivery flyers plastered around Stanford. Evan Spiegel going to the mall handing out Snapchat flyers asking strangers "Hey, would you like to send a disappearing picture?" and getting told "No." Ben Silbermann sneaking into Apple stores, changing all the display computers to show Pinterest, then lurking in the back saying "Wow, this Pinterest thing is really blowing up." Netflix hiring an English major to infiltrate DVD enthusiast forums under a fake identity. Airbnb founders hot-gluing cereal boxes at their kitchen table.

The turning point — the "aha moment" — is the realization that NONE of these strategies scale. That's the whole point. Paul Graham's famous advice, "do things that don't scale," is the through-line. The tactics that get you from 0 to 1,000 are completely different from what gets you to 10,000. You have to go to your users directly — physically, personally, sometimes embarrassingly — before you earn the right to grow through scalable channels. The founders who won were the ones willing to look foolish, do manual labor, and hustle one user at a time.

## Key Numbers & Facts
- Just **7 strategies** account for every major consumer app's early growth
- Most startups found success from **just 1 strategy**, none from more than 3
- DoorDash's first version: a website called paloaltodelivery.com with **PDF menus** charging **$6 for delivery**
- Facebook hit **1,200-1,500 registrants within 24 hours** from a single dorm mailing list of 300 people
- Robinhood's waitlist hit **#3 on Hacker News** on a random Saturday — "Chinese Land Spaceship on the Moon" was #1
- Superhuman's landing page took **2 hours** to build on Squarespace; Rahul Vohra's Mailbox shutdown article took **4 days** and drove **5,000+ signups**
- Buffer acquired **~100,000 users** from **150 guest posts** over 9 months
- Slack had **8,000 invitation requests on day one**, growing to **15,000 in two weeks**
- Spotify launched invitation-only, each user getting only **5 invites** to share
- Mailbox had **700,000 users** in its waitlist queue
- Instagram reached **#1 in camera apps** on launch day thanks to influencer seeding
- Product Hunt started as an **email list** using Linkydink, launched with **30 users** on day one, **100 by end of first week**

## Emotional Core

This piece vibrates with a very specific feeling: the beautiful absurdity of billion-dollar companies starting from nothing. There's deep humor in Evan Spiegel getting rejected at malls, deep scrappiness in DoorDash founders delivering food from PDF menus, deep audacity in Ben Silbermann hijacking Apple Store displays. The emotional tension is between the grandeur of what these companies became and the embarrassing, manual, unglamorous hustle of how they began. Every founder in this article was willing to look foolish, to do things that seemed beneath them, to personally beg strangers one at a time. That's the universal human story: greatness starts with humility. The feeling is aspirational but also comforting — if Tinder started with two people running around a college campus, maybe YOUR crazy idea isn't so impossible.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

Every giant started small, doing embarrassing things that didn't scale — the first thousand users always come from showing up in person, not from a growth hack.
