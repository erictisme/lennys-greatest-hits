## Story Arc

Geoff Charles joins Ramp when it's just 10 people — eight engineers and a dream to take on the corporate card giants. In three months, they build a competitor to Amex. Six months later, they take on Expensify. Both publicly traded billion-dollar companies. But Ramp isn't playing by the normal playbook — no burndown charts, no story points, no two-month planning cycles eating up a third of the year. Their culture has one word: velocity.

The numbers are staggering. Ramp hits $100 million in annual run rate in just two years — the fastest any SaaS company has ever done it. They do it with fewer than 5 PMs and under 50 engineers. When they decide to enter accounts payable, they give three engineers, one designer, and one PM a mandate: build a competitor to Bill.com. Three months. The product is now moving billions of dollars a year. They grow 4x in a single year to $10 billion in annualized spending, during a period when most companies barely grew at all.

The secret isn't working harder — it's a ruthless philosophy: doing is better than planning. Any second you spend planning is a second you don't spend doing. They don't maintain a backlog. They fix every bug the moment it surfaces. They don't track velocity with story points — they track whether you shipped what you said you'd ship. Teams are empowered to the point where the CEO sets the vision but the engineers decide how to get there. And underneath the speed, there's a surprising warmth — a #gratitude Slack channel called "couscous" (named by their early Moroccan team) that's the most popular channel in the company. Because, as Geoff says, taking risks demands humor, kindness, and gratitude.

## Emotional Core

This is a story about what happens when a team truly commits to speed as an identity — not as a slogan, but as the thing that defines who they hire, how they plan (or don't), and what they celebrate. The emotional tension is between the raw, almost reckless velocity and the surprising humanity underneath it. Ramp isn't a burnout factory — it's a place where engineers jump into Slack channels to fix bugs that aren't their job, where gratitude is the most popular channel, where the VP of Product admits PMs are "useless" without their team. There's a David vs. Goliath thrill: ten people going up against Amex, fifty people hitting numbers that Fortune 500 companies dream about. The feeling is exhilarating momentum — like being on a rocket that somehow has a really good vibe inside.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

When speed is your strategy — not just a value on a poster — small teams can beat giants, because every mistake costs less and every lesson comes faster.
