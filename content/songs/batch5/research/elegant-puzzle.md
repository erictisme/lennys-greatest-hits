## Story Arc

Will Larson — CTO at Carta, former eng leader at Stripe, Uber, and Calm, author of *An Elegant Puzzle* and *Staff Engineer* — traces his entire philosophy through the lens of systems thinking and hard-earned failure. The arc begins with the post-ZIRP reckoning: engineering managers who used to spend half their time hiring now do zero interviews a month, and the skills that made you a top performer (hiring well) no longer apply. The turning point is Larson's systems thinking revelation at Stripe: his team got so caught up measuring incidents that they forgot to actually fix anything — "you can't measure infinite times and never cut." The emotional climax is the Digg V4 story: champagne and sushi at launch while the site crashes, 30 days to get it working, everyone quits or gets laid off, Larson becomes eng manager two and a half years into his career because "no one else was dumb enough to take it." The resolution is Larson's hard-won wisdom: boring strategy wins, reality is never wrong (your model is), write where there's energy, and the most talented people often get labeled "hard to work with" because they care too much.

## Specific Numbers & Facts
- Started career right before the 2008 crash; Yahoo had layoffs every 4 months
- At Uber, did 6 interviews back to back in a single day
- Digg V4: site down for ~30 days; team went from ~100 to 30 people in 9 months
- Digg servers had to be restarted every 12 hours due to a Python default parameter bug
- They had to take the roof off a data center in China to crane-lift server racks in (Uber)
- Uber China migration completed in 3 months
- Will has written ~1,000 blog posts over 16 years
- Carta's "standard kit" — one engineering strategy document that constrains tool choice
- Stripe ran a Ruby monolith as deliberate strategy

## Emotional Core
**The beauty of things that work like clockwork — and the courage to keep fixing them when they don't.** Larson sees organizations as systems of stocks and flows, where small bugs cascade and boring constraints create freedom. The Digg story is the emotional anchor: sometimes the elegant puzzle falls apart completely, and you rebuild it piece by piece at 3 AM while everyone else walks away. The people who stay — who keep tuning the system — are the ones who learn the most.

## The ONE Thing
**Great engineering leadership is systems thinking: build the model, compare it to reality, fix what's broken, repeat — and have the courage to do boring, constraining work that nobody celebrates but everyone benefits from.**
