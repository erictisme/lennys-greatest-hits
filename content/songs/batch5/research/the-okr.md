## Story Arc

Christina Wodtke — product leader at LinkedIn, MySpace, Zynga, Yahoo, Stanford lecturer, author of *Radical Focus* — lays out what OKRs actually are and why they fail. The arc moves from "OKRs are a vitamin, not a medicine" through the atomic unit (asking "what am I doing THIS WEEK to get closer?") to the cadence that makes it work (Monday commitments, Friday celebrations). She tells the story of her personal accountability group — three women, five years, weekly check-ins. The most precise tracker burned out and disappeared. The vague hand-waver became a consultant, bought a house, found joy. The lesson: the heart of OKRs isn't measurement precision. It's the weekly ritual of raising your head above the noise and reconnecting with WHY.

The deeper thread: companies fail at OKRs because they rush implementation, confuse tasks with outcomes, spread too thin ("the peanut butter memo"), and lose the human element. The fix is always the same — slow down, start small, celebrate, learn, iterate.

## Specific Numbers & Facts
- Christina has run her personal life on OKRs for 8-10 years (ADHD — needs the focus)
- Lost 25 pounds using the same "simple but hard" principle as OKRs
- Book *Radical Focus* translated into 8 languages; sells best in China (a Chinese actress recommended it)
- Rule of 3 key results: one hard number, one quality/squishy measure, one with a dollar sign
- 70% achievement = success (moonshot calibration)
- OKR planning should take ~4 days max, not weeks
- 70% of Stanford students have significant financial aid (not all rich kids)
- Sweet spot for great product culture: ~250 people

## Emotional Core

This isn't a story about spreadsheets and metrics. It's about the discipline of ATTENTION — the weekly act of asking yourself what matters, doing the work, and then pausing to celebrate what went right. Christina's personal OKR story (ADHD, discovering she drops self-care when busy, the accountability group transforming a hand-waver's entire life) makes this deeply human. The tension is between the organizational machinery of OKRs (which people hate) and the almost spiritual practice of weekly reckoning (which transforms everything).

## The ONE Thing

**The power of OKRs isn't in the measurement. It's in the weekly ritual of stopping, asking what matters, doing the work, and celebrating progress.** The cadence IS the product.
