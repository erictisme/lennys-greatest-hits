## Story Arc

**Beginning:** Pfeffer teaches the most popular (and most uncomfortable) class at Stanford GSB — "Paths to Power." Students enter in denial ("I don't want to be this person"), feeling that power is dirty or manipulative.

**Turning Point:** Through stories, case studies, and the "Doing Power" homework, students move through denial → anger → sadness → acceptance. The reframe: "If you want power to be used for good, more good people need to have power." This isn't about being a villain — it's about being effective.

**End:** The price of power — autonomy vs. power tradeoff. Pfeffer himself chose freedom over institutional power. "You can have power or you can have autonomy, but you cannot have both." James March quote. The dean whose wife was dying but had to attend alumni events. The scrutiny, the loss of privacy, the trust dilemma.

## Emotional Core

**The uncomfortable truth nobody wants to hear:** The world runs on power, not merit. Your brilliant work means nothing if nobody knows who you are. The people who succeed aren't necessarily the smartest — they're the ones who understand influence, visibility, and strategic relationships. And the deepest tension: learning this feels like losing your innocence. You go through grief stages (denial → anger → sadness → acceptance) just from understanding how the world actually works.

**Secondary tension:** Power vs. autonomy. Even Pfeffer himself, who teaches power, chose freedom over it. There's a cost to everything.

---

## The ONE Thing

The world rewards power, not just talent — and power is a learnable skill, not a personality trait. But knowing this truth requires accepting an uncomfortable reality about how things actually work.
