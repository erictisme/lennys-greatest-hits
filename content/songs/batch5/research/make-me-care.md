## Story Arc

Nancy Duarte has spent 35+ years helping companies communicate better — from Al Gore's An Inconvenient Truth to Apple's earliest presentations. She started her company when her husband begged her to believe the Macintosh could be a business. She cold-called NASA, Apple, and Tandem, and won all three contracts at once. The company slipped in during an 18-month window when graphic designers refused to touch the Mac ("It's a toy, it's ugly"). That window made their career.

Her core discovery came from a three-year journey through story. She took quarter-inch graph paper and hand-mapped the greatest speeches in history — Steve Jobs's iPhone launch, MLK's I Have a Dream, the Gettysburg Address. She found the pattern: all great presentations oscillate between "what is" and "what could be," creating longing for a new bliss. She calls this shape the "sparkline."

The deepest insight: the audience is the hero, not the presenter. The presenter is the mentor — Obi-Wan Kenobi, not Luke Skywalker. You bring the lightsaber (a tool for their outer journey) and the force (resolve for their inner journey). Balance of power is always with the audience. They choose whether to accept or reject your idea.

## Emotional Core

The tension between **what is** and **what could be**. Nancy's whole life is this pattern — she saw "what is" (a pixelated Mac) and was told "what could be" by her husband who literally got on his knees to beg her. Every great presentation is a love letter to a future the audience hasn't seen yet. The emotion is not about performance or polish; it's about making someone care enough to move. The real vulnerability: you can't force anyone. The audience always has the power.

## The ONE Thing

**The presenter is not the hero. The audience is.** When you reframe yourself as the mentor — the one who comes alongside, brings a tool, gives courage — everything about your communication changes. You stop performing and start serving.

## 3 Chorus Hook Ideas

1. **"Make me care"** — Sung from the audience's perspective. The challenge to every presenter: I'm sitting here, make me care. Flip it by the final chorus: "I care."

2. **"What is, what could be"** — The sparkline itself as a rhythmic hook. Rising and falling melody that mirrors the oscillation.

3. **"You're not the hero"** — The reframe. Confrontational, memorable. The twist: "They are."

**Recommended:** Hook #1 — "Make me care." It's a dare, a plea, and a transformation arc in three words. The audience starts skeptical and ends transformed. The song IS the sparkline.

## Emotional Direction

This song should feel like a presentation that actually works. Start sparse and uncertain (the "what is" — the boring meeting, the eyes glazing over). Build through the middle with the rise-and-fall sparkline energy. By the final chorus, the room is transformed — people are leaning in. The sonic journey mirrors the emotional journey Nancy describes.

**Tone:** Cinematic folk with orchestral swells. Not triumphant — intimate. Like someone leaning forward in their chair because they finally care.

**Perspective:** Second person — "you" are standing at the front of the room. The song speaks to the presenter, but the hook comes from the audience.
