# Research Brief — Ikigai (Tom Conrad)

## Source
- **Title:** "Billion dollar failures, and billion dollar success | Tom Conrad (Quibi, Pandora, Pets.com, Snap, Zero)"
- **Type:** Podcast
- **Date:** 2023-11-26
- **Guest:** Tom Conrad

## Story Arc

### Act 1: The Kid from Columbus
- 15 years old when the Macintosh came out in Columbus, Ohio
- Only ambition: move to Cupertino and work for Apple
- Heroes were the team who signed their names inside the original Mac case
- Drove his dad's Pontiac from Michigan to California for an Apple internship
- Got hired on the Finder team through sheer luck — a hiring req about to expire

### Act 2: The Spectacular Failures
**Pets.com (1999–2000)**
- Nothing to public company to completely out of business in 19 months
- Three overfunded pet e-commerce sites in an arms race, each raised $50M+
- 80% of the country still on dial-up — "really, really early"
- Didn't go bankrupt — shut down and returned remaining money to investors (first public company to do this)
- The same business model worked perfectly 15 years later (Chewy: $9B company)
- Key line: "An excess of investment can be its own albatross"

**Quibi (2019–2020)**
- Jeffrey Katzenberg & Meg Whitman recruited him for 7 months; he kept declining
- Said he wanted to be a pastry chef, landscape photographer, or make bad music on SoundCloud
- His wife convinced him to try "two days a week" — immediately became six
- Raised over $2 billion, died less than a year after launch
- The problem: content cost a billion dollars a year — "you can't spend two years iterating... you can't afford to not be a hit"
- He's "embarrassed about some of what happened" but "super grateful for the experience"
- It re-lit his enthusiasm for building: "I fell deeply into it right away... just so fun to build a team from scratch"

### Act 3: The Billion-Dollar Success
**Pandora**
- Got introduced to Tim Westergren's Savage Beast (7-8 employees)
- They hired him BECAUSE of Pets.com — "these things do pay dividends, even when they're a disaster"
- CEO Joe Kennedy gave him the keys: "As long as it fits the brief for 1-Click Personal Radio, it's all yours"
- Built it from zero to 80 million users over 10 years
- "Best professional experience that one could have"
- His passion for music drove it: "I was that kid in college who would corner you and be like, if you like this band, I got to play this for you"

### Act 4: The Core Insight
- "My professional satisfaction is not well correlated with those external metrics and very, very correlated with: do I love the thing we were building and do I love the people I was working with?"
- "These things make you better. They became dominoes that opened doors"
- "Whatever the thing is that gets you out of bed every morning, you can achieve that in collaboration with others"
- Not everyone needs to be a founder: "Our industry would be much better off if there were fewer founders"

## Emotional Core
This is a story about a man who kept showing up after the world told him he'd failed. The failures didn't break him — they became the stepping stones. Pets.com got him into Pandora. Quibi reignited his love for building. The Japanese concept of ikigai — finding the intersection of what you love, what you're good at, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for — is what Tom Conrad lived without naming it. His satisfaction came not from the billion-dollar outcomes but from the craft itself and the people beside him.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches
Your failures are not the opposite of your purpose — they are the path to it. The thing that gets you out of bed matters more than the thing that makes you rich.
