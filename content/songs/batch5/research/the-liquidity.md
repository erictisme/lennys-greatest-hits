## Story Arc

**Beginning:** Ben Lauzier joins Lyft as employee #30. They're 30x smaller than Uber — less revenue, fewer drivers, lower liquidity. Existential pressure: everything they do has to be 10x more efficient per person just to survive. They have a waitlist on the demand side because they can't onboard drivers fast enough. 50% of job seekers and welfare recipients in the U.S. don't have a car — that's their supply gap.

**Turning Point:** The concept of liquidity becomes their North Star. Liquidity = how quickly buyers and sellers find each other. For Lyft, the proxy metric is ETA (estimated time of arrival). If the closest driver is under 2 minutes away, riders convert. Over 5 minutes, they check Uber or walk. This single metric becomes the lever everything else connects to. They also discover the mentor program: paying top drivers $35 per session to onboard new drivers. Mentors outperform every marketing email because it's peer-to-peer trust. They scale to match Uber's footprint with 1/10th the resources.

**End:** The GM partnership — renting cars to people who don't have one. Manufacturing supply. Within 18 months, 4th-largest rental fleet in the U.S. But ultimately Lyft's focus on transportation (not food/logistics) costs them during COVID. Uber diversified; Lyft didn't. $150B vs $5B market cap. The lesson: liquidity wins, but only if the marketplace keeps flowing into new channels.

## Emotional Core

The tension between **control and flow**. Marketplace builders sit in ivory towers trying to engineer human behavior, but humans are messy, non-deterministic. The real breakthrough comes from letting go — trusting your community, empowering your best people, letting the marketplace flow like water. Liquidity isn't just a metric; it's a philosophy of abundance through connection.

The deeper human story: Ben at Lyft, 30x smaller than the giant, finding that the competitive advantage wasn't money or scale — it was community and trust. Mentors who said "text me, I'll tell you where the good spot is" outperformed every corporate playbook.

## The ONE Thing

**Trust your people more than your spreadsheet — a marketplace wins when connections flow freely, not when you control every drop.**
