# Research Brief: The Hierarchy

## Source
- **Episode:** The hierarchy of engagement | Sarah Tavel (Benchmark, Greylock, Pinterest)
- **Guest:** Sarah Tavel (Partner at Benchmark, former first PM at Pinterest)
- **Date:** 2023-12-27
- **File:** podcasts/sarah-tavel.md

## Story Arc

**Beginning:** Sarah joins Benchmark from Pinterest, where she led the discovery team. She watches consumer founders obsess over vanity metrics — MAUs, downloads, sign-ups — pretty graphs going up and to the right. But none of it tells her whether these products will endure. It becomes "a pebble in her shoe." She starts writing to distill what actually matters.

**Turning Point:** The three-level hierarchy emerges. Level 1: Find your core action (the ONE thing users do that proves they get the product — pinning for Pinterest, subscribing for YouTube, friending for Facebook). Level 2: Make the product get better the more you use it, so you have more to lose by leaving — "accruing benefits and mounting loss." Pinterest's algorithmic feed got more personalized with every pin. Level 3: Make it self-perpetuating — turn user energy into a flywheel. Every pin creates a new edge in the graph, improving recommendations for everyone. The strongest version is a network effect, but growth loops and re-engagement loops matter too.

**End:** The cautionary tales. Evernote nailed Levels 1 and 2 but had zero Level 3 loops — no way for one user's activity to benefit another. Clubhouse and Houseparty had viral growth but the flywheel broke down — too many push notifications overwhelmed users, and the self-perpetuating engine stalled. She extends the framework to marketplaces: focus on a "thimble" (constrained market), tip the market, then dominate. DoorDash won the suburbs while Postmates spread thin across cities.

## Specific Numbers & Facts
- Pinterest tracked "weekly active pinners" (WAPs) — users who pinned at least once/week
- If a user pinned something, 90%+ probability they'd return next week
- TikTok spent $1 billion on paid user acquisition — but only after nailing retention first
- Pinterest shipped the first algorithmic feed in a social product ("picked for you")
- YouTube's core action evolved from "watching a video" to "subscribing" (insight from CPO Shishir Mehrotra)
- REKKI marketplace: suppliers handed over CSV lists of all their restaurants to onboard — the tipping moment
- Etsy gave sellers physical business cards linking to their store — supply brought demand
- Sean Ellis test: 40%+ of users "very disappointed" if product disappeared = on the right track
- DoorDash focused on suburbs (no competition); Postmates spread across cities + categories
- The more dominant a marketplace is relative to #2, the more profitable it becomes (exponential curve)

## Emotional Core
The hunger for something that lasts. Every founder wants their creation to endure — not spike and crash like Clubhouse, not plateau like Evernote. The hierarchy is a map for building permanence. There's a deep satisfaction in watching a flywheel spin on its own — users creating value for other users, the product getting stronger with every action. And there's a quiet terror in the opposite: watching your beautiful creation collapse because you optimized for the wrong thing.

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches
**Build something that gets stronger every time someone uses it.** Core action → accruing benefits → self-perpetuating flywheel. Each level feeds the next. Skip a level and the whole thing falls apart.

## Chorus Hook Ideas

1. **"Spinning on its own"** — The flywheel metaphor. Each chorus adds another voice, matching the self-perpetuating energy. "Every hand that turns the wheel / Makes it stronger, makes it real / Now it's spinning on its own"

2. **"More to lose"** — The retention hook. Accruing benefits and mounting loss. "Every pin, every save, every name / Something built I can't walk away from / Now I've got more to lose"

3. **"The current pulls you forward"** — Markets as rivers, not lakes. The feeling of momentum building. "I threw my plank into the river / And the current pulls me forward / Every wave a little bigger"

## Suggested Emotional Direction
Start intimate and small — one person, one action, one pin saved at midnight. Build through the levels like the hierarchy itself: verse 1 is the single action, verse 2 is the accumulation (it gets better, you can't leave), verse 3 is the flywheel spinning. The music should BUILD — more voices joining each chorus, matching the congregational energy of CityAlight. By the final chorus, it should feel like a room full of people singing together — the sonic embodiment of a network effect.
