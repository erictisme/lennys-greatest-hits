## Story Arc

**Beginning:** Bob Baxley is a maker who spent his early years hunting for his medium — photographer in high school, aspiring filmmaker in college, music school after that, then a graphic design studio. At 27, he finds software. "I was just hunting for my medium."

**Middle:** 35 years across Apple, Yahoo, Pinterest, ThoughtSpot. At Apple: 6 designers running a store in 30+ countries doing billions in revenue. The patented pizza box in the cafeteria. "How can the thing I'm working on be a little bit better?" Then Pinterest — "I bounced off the culture." Left on Friday, started Monday. No time to recalibrate. Lost his mother while there. Kids starting high school. "These careers are really hard."

**Turning Point:** The realization that software is a medium — not a tool, but a medium like film or music. Driving around Silicon Valley after Pinterest, listening to a podcast about the history of the Valley, he hears a quote: "a very small group of people in and around Stanford University saw software as a new form of media on par with movies, music and books." Everything clicks.

**End:** The moral obligation. Hundreds of interactions with software daily. Watch grandma at the restaurant with the Toast device — will you make her look like a superhero or a fool? "We have an obligation as product people to put that emotional energy back into people's lives." And the Lunar Orbit Rendezvous story — ideas need champions willing to risk everything.

## Emotional Core

This is a story about a lifetime spent caring about craft. Bob isn't a tech bro optimizing metrics — he's a maker who found his medium after years of searching. The emotional weight comes from:

- **The search itself** — photography, film, music, graphic design, then finally software at 27
- **The failures** — Pinterest was hard, his mother died, the culture bounced him
- **The conviction** — after 35 years, he still believes software should make people feel something
- **The tenderness** — watching grandma at dinner with the Toast device, wanting her to look like a superhero
- **The wisdom** — "your career's not your life"

This is a sunset song. A man looking back at 35 years and saying: I found what I was looking for, it cost me more than I expected, and it was worth every minute.

## The ONE Thing

**After a lifetime of making things, the deepest lesson is this: the tools change, the companies change, the titles change — but what matters is whether you showed up with care for the person on the other side of the glass.**
