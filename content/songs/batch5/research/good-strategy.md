# Research Brief: Good Strategy

## Source
- **Title:** Good Strategy, Bad Strategy | Richard Rumelt
- **Type:** Podcast
- **Guest:** Richard Rumelt
- **Date:** 2024-01-21
- **Word Count:** ~14,960

## Story Arc

Richard Rumelt — legend of strategy, former NASA spacecraft designer turned UCLA professor — has spent a lifetime studying why organizations fail at strategy. His core revelation: strategy is not goals, not vision statements, not 17 priorities on a whiteboard. It's a **design for overcoming a high-stakes challenge**. Three elements form the "kernel": diagnosis (what's actually going on), guiding policy (how we'll deal with it), and coherent action (what we'll actually do). The episode builds from the David and Goliath story — strategy as "discovered strength" — through the magnifying glass metaphor (power + focus + target), to the concept of "The Crux" borrowed from rock climbing: the hardest move on the climb, and if you can't do the crux, don't do the climb. He closes with the "foundry" — getting 5-8 smart people in a room to coalesce around an action agenda. Not goals. Actions.

## Specific Numbers & Facts
- Rumelt taught at Harvard and UCLA for decades
- He designed spacecraft for NASA (conceptual designer for what became Voyager)
- One client had **17 priorities** — the opposite of strategy
- **17 different US intelligence agencies** — strategy was "they should work together more effectively"
- Rockefeller dropped kerosene from **$1 to 10 cents** per gallon
- Nokia replaced engineers with lawyers/accountants, lost the smartphone race
- His daughter Cassandra has **25 books** on the NYT Bestseller list
- His company is called "General Imagination" — "like General Motors, but a lot smaller"

## Emotional Core
**The courage to choose.** Strategy is not mysterious — what's mysterious is how many leaders avoid it. They hide behind goals, mission statements, word salad. The real work is the scary part: diagnosing what's actually wrong, choosing ONE thing to focus on, and telling people "we're doing this, not that." It requires the courage to say no, to disappoint people, to concentrate power like sunlight through a magnifying glass. David and Goliath energy — the small, focused force beats the diffused giant.

## The ONE Thing
**Strategy is choosing the hardest problem you can actually solve — then solving it.** Not goals. Not ambitions. Not 17 priorities. One crux. One magnifying glass. One black thread that catches fire.

The magnifying glass finding focus. Jazz-pop sophistication — complex harmonies that resolve into something clean and sure. Jacob Collier meets Tom Misch. Walking upright bass. Rhodes piano.
