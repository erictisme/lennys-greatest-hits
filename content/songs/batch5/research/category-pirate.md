# Research Brief — Category Pirate

**Source:** How to become a category pirate | Christopher Lochhead (podcast, 2023-09-17)
**Guest:** Christopher Lochhead — 13x bestselling author, Godfather of Category Design, 3x public company CMO

---

## Story Arc

**Beginning:** Most entrepreneurs build a "better mousetrap" — they compete inside an existing category, trying to be 10x better on features. VCs blow half a trillion dollars backing these me-too startups. No one cares. One company earns 76% of all the value in a tech category (the "category queen"). Everyone else fights over the remaining 24%.

**Turning Point:** Category design flips this. You don't compete against other products — you compete against the STATUS QUO. You frame, name, and claim a NEW problem. When people see the problem differently, they become open to a new solution. The example that crystallizes everything: Elisha Otis invented the elevator but there was zero demand. He called it "the vertical railway" — languaging that connected the new to the known. Suddenly people could imagine a new category of building: skyscrapers.

**End:** "Positioning is for losers" — positioning means fighting over the 24%. Category pirates don't position themselves against competitors. They CREATE the game. Peloton didn't say "our bike is better than a spinning bike." They said "why risk your life on a road when you can spin at home?" They dammed the demand. The future needs people who CREATE categories, not compete in them.

---

## Key Numbers & Facts

- **76%** — the share of total market cap that the category queen earns in tech categories (not market share — total value)
- **24%** — what everyone else fights over
- **Half a trillion dollars** — what VCs will waste on me-too startups in AI alone in the next five years
- **13** — Lochhead's number of #1 bestselling books
- **80+ years** — how long Gojo Industries (Purell) has been around
- **Zero billion** — what smart VCs call the market size you actually want

## Emotional Core

**Rebellion.** This is a pirate's manifesto — stop playing by the rules of someone else's game. There's a righteous anger here: brilliant people waste their lives building "better" versions of things that already exist, fighting over scraps. The emotional arc is liberation — the moment you stop comparing yourself to the competition and start creating your own game. It's defiant, energetic, almost gleeful.

---

## The ONE Thing This Song Teaches

**Don't compete in someone else's category — create your own.** The biggest strategic mistake is building a better mousetrap. The winners don't fight for position inside existing games. They invent new games and become the only player on the field.
