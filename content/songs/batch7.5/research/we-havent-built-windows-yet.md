# Research Brief: "We Haven't Built Windows Yet"

**Song concept:** The origin story of ChatGPT — from hackathon codebase with a placeholder name to 700M weekly users — told through the lens of awe and scale. The one man who quietly built the most consequential product in human history and still thinks it's just the beginning.

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — "Inside ChatGPT: The fastest-growing product in history | Nick Turley (Head of ChatGPT at OpenAI)" (Aug 9, 2025)

---

## Story Arc

**Beginning — The accidental product**
Nick Turley joined OpenAI when it was still a research lab. His first task was literally fixing the blinds. When they decided to build something consumer-facing, they assembled a volunteer hackathon team — a supercomputing engineer who had once built an iOS app, a researcher who had written some backend code. They were going to call it "Chat with GPT-3.5" because they genuinely didn't think it would be successful. They shipped it right before the holidays thinking they would pull it down after getting some data. They had no waitlist. Sam Altman tweeted it.

**Turning point — The moment the world changed**
The dashboard broke from traffic. Nick thought it was just going viral and would die down. Then people started retaining. He couldn't understand why. They had shipped with no conversation history — that was the first piece of user feedback. The paid subscription tier was priced using a Google Form with the Van Westendorp pricing survey sent to Discord. It was 10 days from decision to ship. The hackathon codebase — SA Server (Super Assistant Server) — became the fastest-growing consumer product in history.

**End — The scale and what's still missing**
700 million weekly active users. 10% of the world's population, every week. 5 million business customers. ChatGPT driving more traffic to Lenny's newsletter than Twitter. And yet — "ChatGPT feels a little bit like MS-DOS. We haven't built Windows yet. And it will be obvious once we do."

- **10 days** from decision to ship ChatGPT

- One-month retention: approximately **90%**; six-month retention: approximately **80%**

- GPT-5 is state-of-the-art on health and coding benchmarks; model is free to all users
- Nick: philosophy + computer science major; was a jazz pianist who almost went to music school instead

## Emotional Core

**The human tension:** The most important product in human history was built by people who didn't know they were building the most important product in human history. There's a profound humility in Nick's voice — not false modesty, but genuine awe. He pinches himself. He takes one day unplugged each week just to process. And even now, with 700M users, he says the Windows moment hasn't arrived yet.

The feeling is **wonder tinged with vertigo** — what does it feel like to stand inside something this enormous and still see mostly the work ahead? It's the feeling of standing at the base of a skyscraper you accidentally built and realizing the blueprint is still on your napkin.

The secondary tension: Nick was almost a jazz pianist. He carries that — improvisation, listening, ensemble play — into how he thinks about product teams. There's something deeply human about the fact that the man who accidentally built MS-DOS for the modern age wanted to spend his life playing jazz.

**Ship before you know what to polish — because the product's real shape only reveals itself when the world gets its hands on it.**

## Emotional Direction

**Mood:** Awe. Reverence. The feeling of watching something enormous from the inside without fully understanding its size. Not triumph — wonder.

**Energy level:** 4/10. This should feel quiet and spacious. The scale of 700 million users is more powerful when whispered than when shouted.

**Genre:** **Indie folk / chamber pop** — acoustic guitar or piano foundation, building to a lush but restrained swell. Think Sufjan Stevens ("Chicago," "Death With Dignity") or early Bon Iver — intimate, detailed, with a moment where the arrangement opens up to mirror the scale of the story. The jazz piano in Nick's personal history is a beautiful texture to bring in — a jazz-inflected folk song would feel uniquely right for a man who almost chose music over tech.

**Why this genre fits:** The story is about accidental greatness. Aggressive or triumphant production would feel wrong — it would impose a "we won" narrative onto a story that is fundamentally about humility and ongoing work. Indie folk/chamber pop holds space for ambiguity. It can feel enormous and intimate at the same time. The quiet, careful guitar that suddenly blooms into strings is the sonic equivalent of "it was supposed to be a hackathon codebase."

**Distinguishing from Electric Dreams:** Electric Dreams covers AI tools broadly. This song is about one specific, staggering human story — the scale (700M weekly users), the accident (10 days, a tweet, a broken dashboard), and the open question (Windows hasn't been built yet). Focus on human awe at civilization-scale consequence, not on AI workflows or productivity. This is a song about what it feels like to be inside something too big to fully see.

---

## Creative Direction Notes

- **Open with the smallness:** Fix the blinds. Send out NDAs. A research lab in early 2022.
- **The name moment is the turning point:** "Chat with GPT-3.5" → Sam tweets it → dashboard breaks → the world changes.
- **Let the numbers land:** 700 million is not a stat — it's 1 in 10 people on Earth, every week. Find the image. (One in ten people you pass on the street. Every street. Every city.)
- **The MS-DOS line is the chorus:** It must feel like a gift — not defeat, but wonder. We're still at the beginning. The Windows moment hasn't come yet. That's terrifying and thrilling.
- **Jazz pianist detail:** Use it. Nick almost chose music. He still plays jazz to decompress. There is something beautiful about the fact that the man who built this thing speaks in musical metaphors — jazz bands, not orchestras. Improvise, listen, riff off each other.
- **Avoid hype:** No "we changed the world" triumphalism. The power is in the restraint. The 10-day ship. The Google Form. The Discord server. These are the details that make it human.
- **End on the open question:** Windows hasn't been built yet. The song should not resolve into celebration. It should end on a horizon — something vast and unfinished, which is exactly where Nick himself stands.
