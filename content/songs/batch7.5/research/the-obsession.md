# Research Brief: "The Obsession"

**Song concept:** The craft of measurement and customer obsession — through Jeff Weinstein's lens as Stripe's product lead
**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — "Building product at Stripe: craft, metrics, and customer obsession | Jeff Weinstein" (July 11, 2024)
**Focus:** MEASUREMENT and OBSESSION (not Stripe the company — three Stripe songs already exist)

---

## Story Arc

**Beginning — The Startup Miss:**
Jeff runs a startup building SQL analytics software. They have a 20-minute outage. The service comes back up. The team high-fives. Customers barely murmur. Jeff doesn't realize until a year later: the silence was the signal. No one was pounding on the door to get their tool back. That silence meant they didn't have product market fit. He calls it "wasting years" on something people didn't urgently need.

**Turning Point — The Atlas Metric:**
Jeff joins Atlas. He looks at the support tickets and sees almost all of them are "sad" — people stuck, confused, frustrated. He asks: "Why would someone recommend Atlas to a friend?" Answer: only if they got through the whole process with zero issues. He creates the metric: **companies with zero support tickets through the entire incorporation flow**. Clean, human, customer-facing. Only 15% were hitting that mark.

**The Obsession:**
Over 18 months, Jeff and a 10-person team drive that number from 15% to 85%. They watch the chart go from a flat crawl to a hockey stick. Market share follows the same curve. They publish the internal dashboard publicly — customers, if they ever saw it, would be "ecstatic" to know that's what Stripe was measuring. The metric becomes the mission. The mission becomes the metric.

**End — The Philosophy Crystallized:**
Jeff's worldview: metrics are a numerical representation of the value you're producing for the customer. If you leaked your dashboard to customers and they weren't thrilled, it's the wrong metric. Count "bad days" — emit a log line every time a customer bumps into a problem. Name your metrics so they make you feel something. Percentages shouldn't have 41 significant digits. The chart must have a heartbeat.

- 55,000 companies started through Atlas to date, generating $5 billion/year in revenue
- Atlas has 80+ NPS with ~50% response rate (Apple is low 60s; AirPods is 75)
- Atlas team: just 10 people
- Support ticket metric: drove from 15% zero-support completions → 85% in 18 months
- More than 25 Study Groups run at Stripe, 250+ participants in just a few months of 2024
- Bug-finder program: 65 videos from a single tweet, dozens of issues found
- Risk review time: improved from long tail → ~100% within an hour
- Jeff "probably could go through my email inbox and just see $1 receipts to random people" — his practice of making founders charge him $1 as practice
- Atlas cohort 2024: startups hit $50 million in revenue twice as fast as 2023 cohort
- More than 20% of multi-founder Atlas teams have co-founders in different countries
- Stripe went from 10 payment methods to 100+, with a flat line period before the hockey stick
- The 83(b) election: 30 calendar days to mail a one-page document to the IRS — missing it has no redo

## Quotable Lines (Lyric Candidates)

1. **"The moment the customer felt compelled enough to go out of their way to talk about some problem, that's an unbelievable gift."**
   — Core of his obsession. Someone spent their finite time on your thing. That's sacred.

2. **"I missed it. During those 20 minutes, our customers weren't furious. That was the signal that we did not have product market fit."**
   — The silence as anti-metric. When no one screams, you're invisible.

3. **"Metrics at their best are a numerical representation of the value we're providing for the customer."**
   — His definition of measurement as a form of empathy.

4. **"If you accidentally leaked your dashboard to your customers, and they would be ecstatic — that's where we're shooting for."**
   — The beauty standard for measurement. Radical transparency as craft.

5. **"I really like charts that go up into the left. Everyone talks about up and to the right. I'm into up and to the left."**
   — Inversion. Obsessive precision. Measuring problems disappearing, not growth accumulating.

6. **"Pick metric titles that make you feel something. That's currency inside a company."**
   — Naming as product design. Metrics as poetry.

7. **"Companies with zero support." That's it. Four words. Not the database field name. Not the underscores and mins and maxes."**
   — Brevity as measurement craft. Simplicity as signal.

8. **"People don't get out of bed for their second problem. They get out of bed for their first problem."**
   — The urgency hierarchy. Only burning counts.

9. **"We emit a log line anytime we think a user bumped into a problem — and we call it a bad day."**
   — The humanizing of data. Emotion baked into infrastructure.

10. **"I will leave a meeting to get one message back to them. Even if it's just: 'Hey, I got this, can I hit you up tomorrow?'"**
    — Responsiveness as obsession made physical.

## Emotional Core

**The human tension:** Most people build things and guess. Jeff refuses. The emotional engine of this song is the vertigo between the internal world (where you have your metrics, your dashboards, your roadmaps) and the external truth (what the customer actually feels). The outage story is the wound: the silence that felt like success was actually failure. The Atlas story is the redemption: a number so simple, so human, so honest, that you could show it to a customer and they'd cheer.

**The feeling this should evoke:** The satisfaction of a craftsperson who found the exact right tool for the job. Not clinical precision — intimate precision. Like a musician who can tune by ear. Like a poet who finds the one word that carries the whole stanza.

**The secondary tension:** Measurement vs. feeling. Numbers vs. tweets. Quant vs. qual. Jeff insists they're not opposites — they're "deep siblings and equals." That tension (and resolution) is the emotional through-line.

## Emotional Direction

**Mood:** Intimate, precise, quietly ecstatic. The feeling of someone who found their religion in data and wants to share it — not evangelizing, just showing you the beauty they see.

**Energy:** 5/10. This is not an anthem. It's a craftsperson's hymn. Controlled intensity. Like watching someone tune a guitar to perfect pitch. Satisfying rather than exhilarating.

**Genre Suggestion:** **Indie folk / neo-soul hybrid** — think Hozier meets Brandi Carlile. Acoustic at the core, with a subtle groove on the chorus. Maybe a minimal fingerpicked guitar verse, then a warm string swell on the chorus. The instrumentation should feel like it has been refined down to only what's necessary — mirroring Jeff's philosophy of the perfect metric: nothing extra, nothing missing.

**Why this genre fits:** Jeff's language is poetic and precise at the same time. He says "metrics at their best" and "numerical representation of value" but he also says "make you feel something" and "heartbeat." That dual register — analytical and deeply human — lives in neo-soul. The acoustic folk keeps it honest. No bombast. The truth needs no production flourish.

---

## Creative Direction Notes

- **Numbers as poetry, not spreadsheets.** Every specific number in this song should feel earned and vivid — 15 to 85, 30 calendar days, one piece of blank paper. Let the specificity be the lyric.
- **The inversion is the hook.** "Up and to the left" is the emotional centerpiece. It's Jeff's worldview encapsulated. Everyone chases growth; he chases the disappearance of failure. That's where the song lives.
- **Silence as a verse theme.** The outage silence. The silence in a customer interview. Sitting long enough to hear "I'll pay you money to solve that." Silence is active, not passive — it should appear as a lyric device.
- **"Bad day" as a refrain candidate.** The warmth of naming a log line a "bad day" — giving human emotion to infrastructure — is the song's most singable concept.
- **Avoid:** technical product jargon, Stripe name-dropping, any verse that sounds like a conference talk. This is a love song to the craft of knowing your customer.
- **The song's emotional resolution:** Not "we shipped it" but "they would have been ecstatic." The customer's imagined joy at seeing the dashboard. That's the payoff.
