# Research Brief: Culture of Excellence

**Source:** "Building a culture of excellence | David Singleton (CTO of Stripe)"
**Date:** 2023-05-04
**Type:** Podcast — Lenny's Podcast
**Song Angle:** Culture as architecture — how you engineer people, not code

---

## Story Arc

**Beginning:** David Singleton joins Stripe — a company that had no PMs for its first ~200 employees and five-plus years. The engineering team was product-minded by design, not by accident. Every early engineer was also acting as a PM.

**Turning Point:** The challenge becomes scale. With thousands of engineers making hundreds of decisions every day, how do you preserve a culture of meticulousness — the thing that made you great — without grinding to a halt? The answer: you turn culture into practice. You make values visible. You create rituals.

**End:** A set of repeatable processes (friction logging, engineer occasions, Walk the Store, UX reviews, the crying octopus button) that operationalize the abstract into the daily. Culture isn't a poster on the wall — it's a bug report form shaped like an octopus emoji.

---

## Specific Numbers & Facts

- Stripe deployed to its core API **16.4 times per day on average**
- Uptime: **99.999%**
- **1 in 10 people in the world** have transacted with a business powered by Stripe
- A code change goes from merge to production in **~45 minutes** (15 min test run, then 30 min auto-deploy)
- Switching to optimized checkout (Payment Element / Stripe Checkout) increased average user revenue by **10.5%** — in an industry where gains are measured in basis points
- Their test suite (the largest distributed system Stripe runs) takes ~15 minutes and runs in parallel
- Stripe error messages have **more code for edge cases than the main flow** — intentional meticulousness
- David reads back through his own engineer-occasion reports **periodically** throughout the year
- Stripe does deep planning **once a year**, lighter planning **mid-year**
- They do a **monthly dev survey** — statistically significant sample, each engineer responds ~once every six months

## Quotable Lines (5+)

1. **"A value matters, but you need to have a practice behind it. That means the value becomes real for everyone."**

2. **"Every engineer at Stripe has many of the attributes you'd often find in PMs in other companies."**

3. **"We are happy to take our time and meet tens or hundreds of people [for a critical hire]. And sometimes we'll meet someone and they're not available right now. And then we'll be patient."**

4. **"If I try to get involved in lots of decisions, everything will grind to a halt. The most important thing is to hire the right people — and then trust them."**

5. **"There are more lines of code in our API handling edge cases than in the main flow. Most people wouldn't do that. But it turns out it's a reason users can adopt our product more quickly and keep adopting other Stripe products."**

6. **"How you show up really does set the culture around you. I try to show up very consistently every day, even though there will very frequently be things that are going wrong and are bad and are hard."**

7. **"Engineer-occasion: I'll clear three or four days, join a team, pick up a small task, get it all the way to production, and keep a friction log. You get to understand the developer tools, the build infrastructure, how long you have to wait — the actual experience."**

8. **"We put a little button in every developer tool at Stripe — an emoji of an octopus that is crying. If you click it, it makes it possible to just type in what's gone wrong. We call those paper cuts."**

## Emotional Core

**The feeling:** The quiet dignity of caring deeply about things most people dismiss — error messages, checkout flows, onboarding UX. The tension between moving fast and staying excellent. The loneliness of holding a high bar when the world rewards speed.

**The human tension:** Every growing company faces the moment when the things that made it special start to erode at scale. The people who care the most feel it first — a standard slipping, a shortcut taken. David's whole framework is about making excellence *structural* so it doesn't depend on personal heroism.

**The resonance:** This is the song for anyone who has ever cared about craft more than their job description required. For the engineer who wrote the perfect error message nobody will notice. For the PM who friction-logged on a Sunday afternoon. For the manager who joined a team as an IC just to feel what the team actually felt.

---

## The ONE Thing

**Excellence is not a value — it's a practice. You have to build systems that make the right behavior the easy behavior, at every layer, every day.**

## Emotional Direction

**Mood:** Quiet conviction. Purposeful. A craftsperson's pride — not loud, not boastful.

**Energy:** 4/10 — slow, considered, steady. This song should feel like someone working at 2am on something they believe in.

**Genre Suggestion:** **Neo-soul / indie folk hybrid** — think Hozier's deliberate pace meets Leon Bridges' warmth. Maybe even a touch of Bon Iver's layered instrumentation in the bridge.

**Why this genre fits:** The subject matter is inherently introspective and architectural — it's about invisible labor, systems of care, the long game. An aggressive genre would betray the ethic being described. Acoustic warmth with soul underneath says: "We do this because we care, not because we're chasing something." The rhythm should feel like methodical work — measured, intentional.

**Suno style tags to use:** acoustic neo-soul, indie folk, warm guitar, intimate vocals, slow build, introspective, handcrafted feel, no EDM elements

---

## Creative Direction Notes

**Frame the song as the song of every person who has ever held a higher standard than their role required.** Not about Stripe, not about David Singleton — about the universal experience of being the person in the room who notices what everyone else walked past.

**Use the concrete images as anchors:**
- The friction log (stream of consciousness notes, alone with a product, noticing what's broken)
- The crying octopus button (absurd and human — someone built this because they cared)
- The error message that links to the doc (small gesture, huge signal)
- Walking the store (the whole company watching a product flow together)
- Engineer vacation — clearing your calendar to actually feel what your team feels

**Avoid:** Making this a management lecture. Keep it personal and grounded. The protagonist is not a CTO — they're someone who loves their craft and is quietly building something that will outlast them.

**Structural suggestion:** Verse 1 introduces the practice (friction log, noticing what others miss). Pre-chorus builds the tension (how do you hold a standard at scale?). Chorus names the truth (excellence is made, not inherited). Verse 2 zooms out to the people who join because they sense this — the hiring-through-mission idea. Bridge goes to the long game: what gets built when people care at every layer.
