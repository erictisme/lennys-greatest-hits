# Research Brief: Coding Is Solved

**Song subject:** Boris Cherny, Head of Claude Code at Anthropic
**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — "Boris Cherny" (published 2026-02-19)
**Album candidate:** Electric Dreams

---

## Story Arc

**Beginning — A little hack in a terminal:**
Boris joins Anthropic after working in Big Tech (Meta/Instagram) wanting to contribute to something mission-driven. He spends a month hacking with no roadmap, builds weird prototypes, posts an internal demo that gets exactly two likes. The thing he built is called "Claude CLI" and it lives in a terminal — not because of grand design vision, but because it was just the easiest way for one person to build alone. Nobody thinks it will matter.

**Rising action — The slow burn:**
Claude Code ships publicly in February 2025. It's not immediately a hit. In May, Boris is still writing 70% of his own code by hand. By May, 30% is AI-generated. He's still using Cursor for most things. The growth curve is there, but it doesn't feel real yet. At a developer conference he predicts engineers won't need IDEs by year's end — the room audibly gasps. He's tracing exponentials that nobody else can see yet.

**Turning point — November:**
In November 2025, Boris crosses 100% AI-generated code. Not by decree — by tracing the curve and letting it arrive. He hasn't edited a single line by hand since. Every day he ships 10, 20, 30 pull requests. During the Lenny recording he has five agents running simultaneously. The Semi-Analysis report drops: 4% of all GitHub commits in the world are authored by Claude Code. Growth is still accelerating.

**End — The builder, not the tool:**
Boris reflects that he has never enjoyed coding as much as he does today — because he doesn't have to do the tedious parts anymore. He draws an analogy to a medieval scribe excited about the printing press because now he can focus on book binding and illustration instead of copying. The deeper lesson: he didn't stop being a builder, he became more of one. He can think about what to build, talk to users, design systems — the parts he always loved. Post-AGI, he says he'd probably make miso.

## Emotional Core

The central human tension is not "AI is replacing coders" — it's the inverse: **a builder who loves building more than ever, precisely because the machine took over the parts he never loved.** Boris doesn't mourn code. He never wanted to write code — he wanted to make things. The code was always just the cost of admission. Now the admission is free.

There's also a quiet irony worth holding: the man who built the tool that "solved coding" is, at his core, someone who learned to code to cheat on a math test, whose grandfather programmed on punch cards in the Soviet Union, and who dreams of making miso in rural Japan. He's not a technologist in love with technology. He's a builder in love with building.

Secondary emotional layer: the pride and humility of someone who shipped a thing that changed the world — and keeps reminding himself how early it still is.

## The ONE Thing

A builder's job was never writing code — it was always figuring out what to make. Coding is solved. Building never will be.

## Emotional Direction

**Mood:** Warm awe. The feeling of someone realizing a dream they didn't know they had. Not triumphant — more like quiet wonder mixed with "I can't believe this is real."

**Energy (1–10):** 6. Mid-tempo. It shouldn't feel like a victory lap. It should feel like someone in the middle of something vast, looking around, a little stunned.

**Genre Suggestion:** Neo-soul / indie folk hybrid — think Hozier meets Sampha. Acoustic guitar foundation with sparse electronic texture creeping in — the human and the machine coexisting in the sound itself. This is the key distinction from the rest of Electric Dreams:

- **Vibe Coding** = playful, tongue-in-cheek, almost comedic
- **Rise of Cursor** = aspirational tech-hype energy
- **The Vibe Coder** = identity / character study

**This song** = quiet revelation. The builder's internal monologue. Introspective. The irony is warm, not sardonic. Boris isn't bragging — he's marveling. The song should feel like someone writing in a journal at 2am with five agents running in the background.

**Why this genre fits:** Neo-soul/folk grounds the song in human feeling even as it describes post-human capability. The genre itself embodies the contradiction: old, honest instrumentation describing something entirely new. That tension is the song's soul.

## Creative Direction Notes

- The title "Coding Is Solved" is the hook, but the song is about a **builder who loves building more**, not less. That's the irony to lean into.
- Avoid making it about the tool (Claude Code). It's about the person. Boris is the protagonist.
- The punch card / printing press analogy is lyric gold — use it. Grandfather programming in the USSR on punch cards, mom drawing on them with crayons: that's a verse.
- "I learned to code to cheat on a math test" — this humanizes him and sets up the arc perfectly.
- The rural Japan / miso detail is the emotional landing. Post-AGI he makes fermented things that take three years. This is not someone who fears the machine — this is someone who knows what he actually loves.
- The Odessa connection (Boris and Lenny both from the same Ukrainian city, neither knew it) is a beautiful moment. Could be woven in as a coda or bridge detail.
- Distinguish from other Electric Dreams songs: **no "vibe coding" slang, no screens glowing in the dark clichés, no hype-bro energy.** This song is quiet, proud, and a little bit philosophically stunned.
- The five agents running during the podcast is a perfect visual for a verse — he's describing the future while living it in real time.
