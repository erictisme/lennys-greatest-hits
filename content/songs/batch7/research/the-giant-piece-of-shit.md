# Research Brief: The Giant Piece of Shit — Stewart Butterfield / Slack

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — "Slack founder: Mental models for building products people love" (Nov 20, 2025)
**Album:** The Craft

---

## Story Arc

**Beginning:** Stewart Butterfield builds two video games (Glitch, and before that the game that became Flickr). Both fail commercially. He's pivoting — not because he's a genius, but because he's run out of good ideas and has to be "coldly rational" about sunk cost.

**Turning Point:** While building Glitch, the team uses IRC for internal communication and keeps adding features to it — "we worked on it for several years without explicitly working on it." They have $9M left. Everyone still loves the game. Stewart shuts it down anyway — humiliating, wrenching, painful. He pivots to the internal tool: Slack.

**End:** Slack launches in February 2014. In the same year, Stewart publicly says it's "a giant piece of shit." That quote gets printed on 40 sheets of paper and pasted on the office wall. The team treats it as a motivational battle cry. Slack becomes one of the fastest-growing B2B SaaS products in history — sold to Salesforce for $27.7B.

The arc: shame as fuel. Discontent as design philosophy. Failure as prerequisite to breakthrough.

## Specific Numbers & Facts

- Slack launched: February 2014
- Stewart born: Dharma Jeremy Butterfield — changed name at age 12
- Remaining capital when Glitch shut down: $9 million
- SLA policy: 100x money back for downtime — proactively applied
- That SLA cost: ~$8 million in revenue credits after a major post-IPO outage
- Threads A/B test: 2.17 vs. 2.14 average messages per thread
- Cal Henderson (CTO): worked together for 23 years
- "Giant piece of shit" quote: MIT Technology Review, 2014
- Direct listing / no lockup for employees at IPO

## Quotable Lines

1. "I feel like what we have right now is just a giant piece of shit. It's just terrible and we should be humiliated that we offer this to the public."
2. "If you can't see almost limitless opportunities to improve, then you shouldn't be designing the product."
3. "In the long run, the measure of our success will be the amount of value that we create for customers." (company chant)
4. "The reason I say you have to be coldly rational about it is because it's fucking humiliating."
5. "Tilt your umbrella." (internal Slack value — empathy for the person coming toward you)
6. "Don't make me think."
7. "Everything is simple if you have no idea what you're talking about."
8. "Your failure to really be considerate and empathic about other people's experience is an advantage that you can create."
9. "Your margin is my opportunity." (Bezos, cited approvingly)

## Emotional Core

Divine discontent. The ache of knowing something could be better and refusing to stop. Pride underneath the self-flagellation — the guy who calls his own product a piece of shit is the same guy who made it great.

**Human tension:** You have to be proud enough to ship and humble enough to hate what you shipped. The pivot is shameful — you promised people something and it failed — but staying is worse.

---

## The ONE Thing

True builders are never satisfied — not because they're broken, but because they can see what's possible. Embarrassment is a compass.
