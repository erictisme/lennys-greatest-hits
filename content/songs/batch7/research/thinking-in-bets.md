# Research Brief: Thinking in Bets — Annie Duke

**Source:** Lenny's Podcast — Annie Duke interview (May 2, 2024)
**Album:** Deep Work

---

## Story Arc

**Beginning:** Annie walked into VC firms after Thinking in Bets came out. Heard the same thing: "Our feedback loops are a decade. What you do doesn't apply to us."

**Turning Point:** She challenged the premise: "Do you invest and then go to sleep like Rip Van Winkle?" Showed firms like First Round Capital that there is no such thing as a long feedback loop — you can always find leading indicators.

**End:** After five years embedded at First Round, they have structured decision rubrics, partner-level scoring, and actual calibration data. Stewart Butterfield's story caps it: he quit Glitch, rolled the money into Slack. The real cost of not quitting is opportunity cost you can't see yet.

---

## Specific Numbers & Facts

- Annie won over $4 million in poker tournaments
- Won a World Series of Poker Bracelet
- Structured hiring interviews improved hit rate from ~50% to 65% (Kahneman research)
- Glitch had $6 million in the bank, 5,000 diehard users
- For every diehard Glitch user, 95-99 came for five minutes and left
- After paid marketing, growth was 6-7% week over week
- Glitch break-even math: 31 weeks at acquisition cost
- Butterfield shut down Glitch November 2012, pivoted two days later to Slack
- Bezos's 70% rule: make decisions with 70% of the info you wish you had

## Emotional Core

The quiet tension between wanting certainty and needing to act before you have it. The shame of being wrong. The sunk cost trap. The liberation of making things explicit: once you name what you believe, you can find out if you're right.

---

## The ONE Thing

You don't have a long feedback loop — you have an excuse to avoid finding out you're wrong. Name your bets, track your signals, shorten the loop.
