# Research Brief: Culture Code

## Story Arc

**Beginning:** Elizabeth Stone arrives at Netflix as VP of Data and Insights — an economist by training, an unusual path to tech leadership. She enters a company obsessively built around one idea: if you fill a room with extraordinary people and give them freedom, they'll do extraordinary things.

**Middle:** The culture demands things that "don't feel like natural human behavior." You have to tell people the truth — even when they've poured their heart into something. You have to ask yourself "the keeper test" — would I fight to keep this person? You have to let people go with dignity when the answer is no. Elizabeth rises from VP to CTO, carrying the weight of maintaining this culture at scale: setting the example, giving the hard feedback, jumping in with both feet to help people close the gap.

**Turning Point:** The tension isn't cruelty vs. kindness — it's that real care looks uncomfortable. Elizabeth's mother told her "the last 5% is the 5% that really matters." Excellence isn't about long hours; it's about holding yourself to a standard that most people avoid because it's hard.

**End:** The payoff is a team where the best people want to stay because they're surrounded by the best people. Where feedback feels safe because everyone's on the same team. Where "something good happens every day" — her mother's other lesson — because you built something worth being part of.

## Emotional Core

The human tension is between CARE and COMFORT. Genuine care for people means telling them the truth even when it's uncomfortable. The easy path is to avoid the hard conversation, let mediocrity slide, and call it "being nice." The Netflix way says: real kindness is helping someone be their best, even if it stings in the moment. The weight Elizabeth carries is doing this at scale — hundreds of people, every day, holding the line.

## The ONE Thing
The hardest kind of love is the kind that tells you the truth.
