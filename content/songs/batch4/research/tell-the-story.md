## Story Arc

**Beginning:** Andy Raskin is a coder in the dot-com era. He and a friend build a Windows app, start getting users, and decide to pitch investors. Andy writes the pitch. A VC rates it "1 (Worst)" and scrawls in the margin: "Not a compelling story."

**Turning Point:** Weeks later, Andy walks past a Barnes & Noble. A sign reads: "For anyone who wants to tell a compelling story." It points to screenwriting books. He reads them. He realizes: a movie is a pitch. Star Wars is a pitch for "be good, trust the Force." He restructures the investor pitch using movie storytelling principles. They get a term sheet months later. "We didn't change the product. It was basically the same business — just how we talked about it."

**Middle:** Andy spends 10-15 years not believing CEOs would "budget a line item for the story." Eventually writes "The Greatest Sales Deck I've Ever Seen" on Medium — 2 million views. That proves him wrong. He builds a career helping CEOs craft strategic narratives.

**End:** The five-part framework: (1) Name the shift — old game to new game, (2) Name the stakes — show winners already playing, (3) Name the object of the new game — the rallying cry, (4) Show the obstacles, (5) Offer the magic gifts to overcome them. Companies like Salesforce, Zuora, Gong, and Drift all used this structure to break away from competition.

## Emotional Core

The tension is between **substance and story** — the belief that a good product should sell itself vs. the reality that how you frame something IS the thing. Andy's personal journey embodies this: same product, different story, completely different outcome. There's vulnerability in admitting that the "rational" pitch failed and the "story" pitch worked. And there's a quiet reckoning: most people still pitch like the arrogant doctor, listing features, comparing specs, when what people actually respond to is being invited into a movement.

**Human tension:** We all have something worth saying but struggle to make others care. The gap between what you've built and how you talk about it.
