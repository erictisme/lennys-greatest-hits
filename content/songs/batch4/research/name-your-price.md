## Story Arc

**Beginning:** You built something valuable. You poured everything into the product — features, design, delight. But when it came time to put a number on it... you froze. You plucked a price out of thin air, or worse, you gave it away for free. Pricing felt uncomfortable, nebulous, political. So you ignored it.

**Middle:** The neglect compounds. You're leaving money on the table. You're cheapening your own product. Customers attribute a $0 value to what you built. You have no feedback loop on what people will actually pay. Patrick Campbell calls pricing "the nexus of uncomfortable and long-term" — it's the growth lever nobody touches. You have three levers: acquire, monetize, retain. You're spending all your time on acquisition, some on retention, and doing absolutely nothing on monetization.

**Turning Point:** The realization — your price is just the exchange rate on the value you create. It's not a number you set once. It's a living system. The most successful companies optimize monetization every quarter. Not changing the price every 3 months — but touching something: packaging, add-ons, localization, the value metric itself.

**End:** The unlock — find your value metric (what you charge for, not how much). Get that right and everything else follows. Churn drops 20-25%. Expansion revenue doubles. Small customers pay small prices, big customers pay big prices, and everyone feels the value matches. Revenue per customer goes up and to the right. Just do something, once a quarter. Rip the bandaid off.

## Specific Numbers & Facts

- Patrick Campbell bootstrapped ProfitWell and sold it for over **$200 million** with no funding
- Companies using value metrics grow at **double the rate** with **half the churn** and **2x expansion revenue**
- Revenue per customer is **30% higher** when you just use proper local currency symbols
- Value propositions swing willingness to pay **+/- 20%** in B2B
- Design boosts willingness to pay by **20%**
- Discounting over **20%** correlates almost perfectly with higher churn
- Case studies boost willingness to pay **10-15%**
- Most companies don't change their actual price for **3 years**
- If NPS is over **20**, you should raise prices once per year
- Evernote waited **many years** before overhauling pricing (Naomi was there)
- Three growth levers: **acquire, monetize, retain** — most companies do nothing on #2
- Rolls Royce charges per mile for airplane engines — they own the engines on the plane you own

## Emotional Core

**The feeling:** That cringe of knowing you should charge more but being afraid to ask. The vulnerability of putting a number on your work. The slow-dawning horror of realizing you've been giving away value for years. And then — the quiet confidence that comes from finally naming your price.

**The human tension:** We'll obsess over every pixel of our product but avoid the one conversation that actually sustains us. Pricing feels like asking someone to value YOU, not your product. It's deeply personal, deeply uncomfortable, and deeply necessary.

---

## The ONE Thing
Stop avoiding the uncomfortable conversation about what your work is worth — your price is just the exchange rate on the value you create, and you're overdue to raise it.
