## Story Arc
Nir Eyal spent five years writing a book about distraction — because he was so distracted. He tried everything: bought a flip phone from Alibaba, a word processor off eBay, stripped away all technology. And he still got distracted — cleaning his desk, taking out the trash, picking up a book. The breakthrough: the problem was never the technology. It was his inability to sit with discomfort. 90% of distraction comes from internal triggers — boredom, loneliness, fatigue, anxiety — not pings and dings. The opposite of distraction isn't focus; it's traction. Both words share the same Latin root (trahare = to pull) and end in A-C-T-I-O-N. Traction pulls you toward what you planned. Distraction pulls you away. The difference is one word: intent. His 10-minute rule: when tempted to escape, set a timer. You can do anything for 10 minutes. Emotions are waves — they crest and subside. His mantra: "This is what it feels like to get better."

## Emotional Core
The relief of realizing nothing is broken. The discomfort isn't the enemy — it's the signal. Everyone from Plato's time to ours has felt this same pull away from what matters. The empowerment of discovering that distraction is an action you take, not something done to you. The quiet courage of sitting with an itch for 10 minutes and watching it dissolve.
