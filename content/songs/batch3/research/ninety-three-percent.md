# Research Brief: Ninety-Three Percent

## Source
- **Title:** How to pass any first-round interview (even in a terrible talent market)
- **Author:** Coach Erika (Erika Gemzer), guest post on Lenny's Newsletter
- **Date:** July 4, 2023
- **Type:** Newsletter (free edition)

## Story Arc
Coach Erika, an engineer-turned-PM-turned-CPO, coached 200+ tech job seekers. 93% landed at top-tier companies (Google, Meta, Uber, Airbnb, Stripe). Her secret: Minimum Viable Interview Prep (MVIP) — a systematic but not exhausting approach. The arc moves from understanding WHY employers interview (filtering, not comprehensive assessment) to HOW to prepare: know your story, walk down memory lane, master behavioral frameworks (STAR++), and formulate questions that make interviewers think.

## Key Facts & Numbers
- 93% success rate coaching 200+ job seekers
- 3-5 major projects to prepare (not hundreds of questions)
- 70% of behavioral questions are "pure" (tell me about a time...)
- 55% more effective than old interview methods (behavioral questions)
- 3-4 days prep at 4 hours/day, or 1 week at 2-3 hours/day
- 10% intro / 70% interviewer questions / 20% your questions (time breakdown)
- Behavioral questions invented 50 years ago (1970s)

## Quotable Lines / Song-Worthy Phrases
1. "Memories aren't stable — they experience neural bit rot"
2. "Interviews are often won or lost by the questions YOU ask"
3. "One single, decisive reason will do"
4. "Use their language when describing your experiences" (mirroring)
5. "Walk down memory lane" — recall every detail of 3-5 projects
6. "Run if a company gives you an offer at this stage"
7. "Get them thinking (in a good way)"
8. "Not burning themselves to the ground endlessly preparing, or winging it"

## Emotional Core
The nervous energy of preparation meeting calm confidence. The transformation from anxiety ("what if they ask something I don't know?") to readiness ("I know my stories, I know my worth"). It's about the ritual of getting ready — the night before, the mirror practice, the index cards — and the quiet power that comes from doing the work.

## THE ONE THING
You don't need to prepare for every possible question — you need to know your own story so well that any question becomes a doorway back to what you've already lived through. Preparation isn't memorization; it's remembering who you are.
