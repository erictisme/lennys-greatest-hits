# Research Brief: Make Time

## Source
- **Podcast:** "Making time for what matters | Jake Knapp and John Zeratsky (authors of Sprint and Make Time, co-founders of Character Capital)"
- **Date:** February 11, 2024
- **Guests:** Jake Knapp + John Zeratsky
- **Word count:** 18,414

## Story Arc
Jake Knapp and John Zeratsky spent a decade "designing time" through design sprints at Google Ventures — controlling how teams work hour-by-hour to achieve big goals in short periods. They started applying sprint principles to their own daily lives and discovered something counter to all productivity advice: it's not about getting faster at what's in front of you. It's about putting the important thing first and building everything else around it. Their framework centers on one question: "If someone asked you at the end of the day, what was the highlight of your day — what would you want to say?" That's the anchor. Pick one highlight. Protect 60-90 minutes for it. Everything else can be a mess, and you still feel good. The climactic story: Jake's 12-year-old son bursts in during peak focus time — "Do you want to sled?" Jake nearly says no (his highlight was podcast prep), then realizes: they rarely get snow, his son is about to be a teenager, there may never be another day his kid wants to wrap arms around him and go down a hill. He goes. It's magical. The highlight shifted. The day was a B- on productivity but an A+ on life.

## Key Facts & Numbers
- 60-90 minutes: the block needed to get into the zone on a highlight
- 87 tactics in the Make Time book
- Jake gives himself a B to B- most days, sometimes C or D, sometimes A
- Jake's son is 12, on the cusp of being a teenager
- They get significant snowfall once every two years on their island
- John does focus time Monday, Wednesday, Thursday mornings
- The "busy bandwagon" + "infinity pools" = bad flywheel
- 3 highlight strategies: urgency, satisfaction, joy

6. "Things can sometimes be a mess outside of that, and you still feel really good about your days."

9. "Even if you don't see it again, just writing it down is a big deal."
10. "It's not really about productivity. It's really just about, look, at any given day, we're lucky if we can have one great moment."

## Emotional Core
The permission to have a mess of a day and still call it good — if the one thing that mattered got its moment. The tenderness of a father choosing a sled ride over a focus block. The relief that productivity isn't about doing everything; it's about doing the one thing. The quiet rebellion against the busy bandwagon.

## THE ONE THING
You don't need a perfect day. You need one moment you chose on purpose.
