# Research Brief: The Stack Rank

## Sources
- "How to communicate tradeoffs so leaders will listen" by Tara Seshan (newsletter, 2024-09-10)

## Story Arc
Tara Seshan spent years failing to communicate tradeoffs. She'd present pros and cons, and leadership would always say "do both." She'd end up burning out her team. Counterproductive phrases like "We are thin on resources" or "Maybe we can sneak it in during spare cycles" actually invited leaders to avoid the hard choice. When she finally learned how to frame tradeoffs correctly, the first time it worked was "exhilarating — we only had to do the one most important thing!"

Her 5 techniques: (1) Repetition doesn't spoil the prayer — create an Ongoing Stack Rank (OSR) and present it everywhere. She once mapped top 10 features to the Billboard Top 10 and wrote song lyrics for each ("Metered billing-oh-na-na" to Havana). Cringe but unforgettable. (2) Steelman the request — the Amazon screws story: a PM got Bezos's famous "?" email about screws being hard to find. Instead of dismissing it, the PM dug in and discovered the real issue: anything unbranded and specification-driven was hard to find. Screws were just a symptom. (3) Company first, team second — at Stripe Billing, Tara couldn't get requests prioritized until she reframed from team goals to company goals (global expansion). (4) Predict the future — Steve at Watershed proposed automating carbon measurement instead of hiring analysts. He showed a detailed 3- and 15-month outlook and gave it a catchy name: "Transform Measurement!" (5) Always communicate an opinionated decision using SCQA framework.

Three traps: the peanut butter trap (spread too thin on tiny requests), the "just one more engineer" trap (Google Video vs YouTube — "I think we just need one more good Java/UI engineer"), and the "but we have a framework" trap.

## Emotional Core
The relief of clarity. Every PM knows the suffocating feeling of "do both" — the impossible pile, the late nights, the team morale tanking. This song is about the courage to draw a line and say "we can do this OR that, not both" — and the liberation when leadership actually makes the call.

## THE ONE THING
The hardest thing in product isn't building — it's choosing what not to build and making that choice stick.
