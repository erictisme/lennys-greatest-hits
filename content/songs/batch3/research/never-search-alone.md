# Research Brief: Never Search Alone

## Source
- **Title:** Land your dream job in today's market: negotiation tactics, job search councils, and more
- **Guest:** Phyl Terry (Author, "Never Search Alone")
- **Type:** Podcast
- **Date:** 2024-09-12

## Story Arc
Phyl Terry's mother formed a council of teachers in 1960 in the San Fernando Valley. That group met for 50 years — until the year she died. In 1976, Phyl's dad left the family. His mom, a single middle-aged woman who'd lost her teaching tenure, had to restart as an entry-level teacher in mid-'70s Los Angeles. But she had her council. They held her hand through it.

Phyl carried this into tech. After the dotcom crash, he helped hundreds of people navigate job searches. He formalized the method into Job Search Councils: groups of 6-8 job seekers who commit to supporting each other. Over 2,000 councils launched, completely free, 20,000+ hours of volunteer work. The average council job search takes 3 months vs the national average of 3-6 months.

The core psychological insight: when you put anxious people together and ask them to be vulnerable, it flips fear into hope, motivation, and confidence. In a world of increasing loneliness, these councils give people real community — not message boards, real community.

## Key Facts & Numbers
- Mother's council met for 50 years (1960-2010)
- 6-8 members per council
- 2,000+ councils launched
- 20,000+ hours of volunteer time
- Average search: 3 months (vs national 3-6 months)
- Councils are 100% free
- 2,000 moderators trained
- "Fast seekers" meet twice a week; "slow seekers" every two weeks
- Book sales + personal funds cover all costs
- "Meeting zero" — first meeting focused on vulnerability and trust

## Emotional Core
The loneliest, most shame-filled experience in professional life — losing your job — transforms when you stop hiding and let strangers hold your hand. Vulnerability shared becomes strength multiplied. This isn't career advice dressed as community; it's community that happens to fix careers.

## THE ONE THING
Anxiety shared becomes hope — the psychological alchemy of opening up to strangers during your most vulnerable professional moment.
